\chapter{Discussion}
\label{chap:discussion}

The sensitivity estimates of Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity_comparison} rest on a small set of modelling simplifications that bound their interpretation. The remainder of this chapter addresses the linear-interference omission (Section~\ref{sec:disc_interference}), the absence of systematic uncertainties (Section~\ref{sec:disc_systematics}), and the deferred choice between the three discriminants (Section~\ref{sec:disc_discriminant}).

\section{Interference between the SM and BSM amplitudes}
\label{sec:disc_interference}

The signal samples include only the quadratic EFT term $d^{2}|\mathcal{M}_{8}|^{2}$, while the linear interference term $2d\,\Re(\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{SM}}^{*}\mathcal{M}_{8})$ is neglected. The quadratic term is positive definite and depends only on the magnitude of the Wilson coefficient, whereas the linear term changes sign with $d$ and must be modelled per operator. The omission is not uniform across the three operator families, since its size tracks the polarisation structure of Chapter~\ref{chap:sm}.

Scalar operators excite only longitudinal modes and therefore share the helicity structure of the SM quartic amplitude, producing the largest linear interference. The quadratic-only model is consequently the least complete on the family with the smallest absolute sensitivity, $Z_A=1.2$ on $S_0$ (Section~\ref{sec:sens_scalar}). Mixed operators lie between the two extremes: their longitudinal component contributes a reduced linear interference, while the transverse component contributes through the quadratic term alone. Tensor operators are orthogonal to the SM amplitude in helicity space, so their linear interference is suppressed. The quadratic-only approximation is therefore most accurate on the family with the largest absolute sensitivity.

A change that should be made to the analysis regardless of these family-dependent differences is to include the linear-interference contribution in the training and evaluation samples, rather than the quadratic-only model used throughout this thesis. The primary motivation is accuracy. The quadratic-only samples are an incomplete signal model, and the size of the omitted linear term is itself family-dependent: large for scalar operators, intermediate for mixed operators and strongly suppressed for tensor operators. Restoring it yields a substantially more accurate signal model, and hence a more reliable sensitivity estimate, for every operator. Because the interference enters with a family-dependent size and shape, training on quadratic and linear samples would also expose the network to some additional information correlated with the operator family. It should be stressed, however, that this is not expected to resolve the mixed/tensor degeneracy of Section~\ref{sec:multiclass_classification}: the interference is a subdominant component of the signal in the high-mass tail that drives the sensitivity, and the difference between the intermediate mixed and the suppressed tensor interference is correspondingly small, so it is unlikely to separate two families that the dominant quadratic terms already leave degenerate. The realistic expectation is therefore a far more accurate picture of each operator, not a clean separation of the degenerate families. Including the linear contribution is nonetheless the natural next step for the analysis, ahead of any attempt to tag the boson polarisation directly, whose experimental feasibility in the fully hadronic channel is far from established.

\section{Remaining systematic uncertainties}
\label{sec:disc_systematics}

The Asimov significances reported in this thesis include a single per-bin Gaussian background normalisation uncertainty of $30\%$, entering Equation~\ref{eq:zasymptotic} as $\sigma_{b,i}=0.30\,b_i$. This single nuisance is a deliberately conservative envelope of the per-process normalisation uncertainties of the backgrounds in the training selection; since that selection is approximately $96\%$ QCD multijet, it is in practice dominated by the large and difficult-to-model QCD multijet normalisation. It is, however, a normalisation-only term: no shape uncertainty, and none of the remaining experimental or theoretical systematics, is modelled in the present $Z_A$.

The dominant uncertainties not yet treated are the large-$R$ jet energy and mass scale and resolution, which directly distort the input features the discriminant relies on most heavily, the leading signal-jet $p_T$ and the two signal-jet masses (Section~\ref{sec:feature_ranking}), and the per-process modelling of the subdominant $V$+jets, $t\bar{t}$ and diboson backgrounds, whose normalisations are subsumed into the single envelope above rather than propagated as individual nuisance parameters, and whose shape uncertainties are not represented at all. On the signal side, the renormalisation and factorisation scales, the parton distribution functions and the parton-shower matching contribute additional uncertainties that propagate as both normalisation and shape distortions. A realistic measurement would profile each of these effects as a separate nuisance parameter, replacing the single $30\%$ envelope with per-process normalisation and shape terms and adding the experimental and signal-theory systematics that the present $Z_A$ omits.

The three discriminants are not expected to respond identically to a full profiling. Because its softmax output must share probability across the four classes, the multi-class discriminant is less sharply peaked than the binary logit, its signal pile-up sitting one to two bins lower in score (Section~\ref{sec:sens_discriminants}), so a larger share of its sensitivity lies in bins with more background and a larger relative background uncertainty. It is consequently the architecture most exposed to the per-process normalisation and shape nuisances, and its sensitivity ranking relative to the binary models could shift once those nuisances are introduced.

\section{Choice of final analysis discriminant}
\label{sec:disc_discriminant}

At heart the comparison of Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity_comparison} is not a search for a single best network but a trade-off between analysis complexity and precision. The specialised classifiers deliver the highest precision: they reach the largest absolute $Z_A$ on the scalar and mixed families and remain competitive on the tensor family, where the only model to surpass them is the global binary classifier, and then only because the v3 selection penalises the specialised model (Section~\ref{sec:sens_tensor}). This precision is bought with the highest complexity, three independently trained networks each undefined outside its training pair, so that the operator family under test must be assumed before a discriminant can even be applied. The global binary classifier sits at the opposite pole: a single network covers the full nineteen-operator basis and is by far the simplest object to calibrate and maintain, at the price of $29\%$ to $32\%$ of the specialised significance on the rare scalar family, while matching the specialised model within $\pm 5\%$ on the mixed family and exceeding it on the tensor family. The multi-class classifier is likewise a single network and additionally exposes the operator family in its output, but is the least precise of the three on the mixed and tensor families, $31\%$ to $39\%$ below the specialised model on the mixed family and $7\%$ to $21\%$ below on the tensor family; on the scalar family it loses $25\%$ to $27\%$, in fact edging ahead of the global classifier there. Its family-identification capability is moreover limited by the mixed/tensor degeneracy of Section~\ref{sec:multiclass_classification}, and the linear-interference training extension of Section~\ref{sec:disc_interference}, while it would sharpen the signal model and add some family information, is not expected to turn that output into a clean family identifier.

A definitive choice between the three architectures requires the systematic-uncertainty profiling of Section~\ref{sec:disc_systematics}, deferred to the analysis proper, and is therefore not made in this thesis. What the comparison does fix is the form of that decision: the analysis must weigh the higher precision of the specialised models, paid for with a family of dedicated networks and the assumption of the operator under test, against the lower complexity of a single global network that spans the entire operator basis and gives up meaningful precision only on the scalar family, which sits well below discovery in any case.
