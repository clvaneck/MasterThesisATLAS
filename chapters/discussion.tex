\chapter{Discussion}
\label{chap:discussion}

The sensitivity estimates of Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity_comparison} rest on a small set of modelling simplifications that bound their interpretation. The remainder of this chapter addresses the linear-interference omission (Section~\ref{sec:disc_interference}), the absence of systematic uncertainties (Section~\ref{sec:disc_systematics}), and the deferred choice between the three discriminants (Section~\ref{sec:disc_discriminant}).

\section{Interference between the SM and BSM amplitudes}
\label{sec:disc_interference}

The signal samples include only the quadratic EFT term $d^{2}|\mathcal{M}_{8}|^{2}$, while the linear interference term $2d\,\Re(\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{SM}}^{*}\mathcal{M}_{8})$ is neglected. The quadratic term is positive definite and depends only on the magnitude of the Wilson coefficient, whereas the linear term changes sign with $d$ and must be modelled per operator. The omission is not uniform across the three operator families, since its size tracks the polarisation structure of Chapter~\ref{chap:sm}.

Scalar operators excite only longitudinal modes and therefore share the helicity structure of the SM quartic amplitude, producing the largest linear interference. The quadratic-only model is consequently the least complete on the family with the smallest absolute sensitivity, $Z_A=1.2$ on $S_0$ (Section~\ref{sec:sens_scalar}). Mixed operators lie between the two extremes: their longitudinal component contributes a reduced linear interference, while the transverse component contributes almost completely through the quadratic term. Tensor operators are orthogonal to the SM amplitude in helicity space, so their linear interference is suppressed. The quadratic-only approximation is therefore most accurate on the family with the largest absolute sensitivity.

A change that should be made to the analysis regardless of these family-dependent differences is to include the linear-interference contribution in the training and evaluation samples, rather than the quadratic-only model used throughout this thesis. Because the interference enters with a family-dependent size and shape, training on quadratic and linear samples would also expose the network to some additional information correlated with the operator family.

\section{Remaining systematic uncertainties}
\label{sec:disc_systematics}

The Asimov significances reported in this thesis include a single per-bin Gaussian background normalisation uncertainty of $30\%$, entering Equation~\ref{eq:zasymptotic} as $\sigma_{b,i}=0.30\,b_i$. It is, however, a normalisation term, no shape uncertainty and none of the remaining experimental or theoretical systematics, is modelled in the present $Z_A$.

The dominant uncertainties not yet treated are the large-$R$ jet energy and mass scale and resolution, which directly distort the input features the discriminant relies on, the leading signal-jet $p_T$ and the two signal-jet masses (Section~\ref{sec:feature_ranking}). On the signal side, the renormalisation and factorisation scales, the parton distribution functions and the parton-shower matching contribute additional uncertainties that propagate as both normalisation and shape distortions. A realistic measurement would profile each of these effects as a separate nuisance parameter, replacing the single $30\%$ envelope with per-process normalisation and shape terms and adding the experimental and theoretical systematics that the present $Z_A$ omits.

\section{Choice of final analysis discriminant}
\label{sec:disc_discriminant}

At heart the comparison of Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity_comparison} is not a search for a single best network but a trade-off between analysis complexity and precision. The specialised classifiers deliver the highest precision: they reach the largest absolute $Z_A$ on the scalar and mixed families and remain competitive on the tensor family, where the only model to surpass them is the global binary classifier. This precision is bought with the highest complexity, three independently trained networks, so that the operator family under test must be assumed before a discriminant can even be applied. The global binary classifier sits at the opposite pole: a single network covers the full nineteen-operator basis and is by far the simplest object to calibrate and maintain, at the price of $29\%$ to $32\%$ of the specialised significance on the rare scalar family, while matching the specialised model within $\pm 5\%$ on the mixed family and exceeding it on the tensor family. The multi-class classifier is likewise a single network and additionally exposes the operator family in its output, but is the least precise of the three on the mixed and tensor families, $31\%$ to $39\%$ below the specialised model on the mixed family and $7\%$ to $21\%$ below on the tensor family. On the scalar family it loses $25\%$ to $27\%$, edging ahead of the global classifier. Its family-identification capability is moreover limited by the mixed/tensor degeneracy of Section~\ref{sec:multiclass_classification}.

A definitive choice between the three architectures requires the systematic-uncertainty profiling of Section~\ref{sec:disc_systematics}, deferred to the analysis proper, and is therefore not made in this thesis. What the comparison does fix is the form of that decision: the analysis must weigh the higher precision of the specialised models, paid for with a family of dedicated networks, against the lower complexity of a single global network that spans the entire operator basis and gives up meaningful precision only on the scalar family, which sits well below discovery in any case.
