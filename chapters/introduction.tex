\markboth{INTRODUCTION}{Introduction}
The effort to understand the fundamental structure of matter has driven scientific progress since the start of civilisation. Ideas about the composition of matter have grown into the quantitative field of particle physics. Modern particle physics combines quantum field theory with large scale accelerator experiments capable of probing interactions beyond those accessible in any other scientific context. The most accurate framework describing all known elementary particles and their interactions is the Standard Model (SM). At the centre of testing this framework is CERN, where high-energy proton beams are brought into collision to study rare and energetic processes.

CERN operates a chain of accelerators that step by step increase the proton energy before injection into the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). The LHC currently delivers proton-proton collisions at a centre of mass energy of $13.6\,\mathrm{TeV}$ at four detector collision points. ATLAS and CMS are general purpose detectors designed to study a wide range of physics. LHCb focuses on heavy flavour dynamics, while ALICE studies the quark gluon plasma created in heavy ion collisions. The work presented in this thesis was done as part of the ATLAS collaboration.

This thesis concentrates on vector boson scattering (VBS), a rare electroweak process in which pairs of W or Z bosons are produced in association with two forward jets. The electroweak sector of the Standard Model predicts self interactions between gauge bosons through triple and quartic gauge couplings. Potential modifications of these gauge interactions, for example through anomalous quartic gauge couplings, can alter the scattering kinematics and lead to observable deviations from Standard Model predictions.

VBS signatures can be studied in several decay channels. This thesis examines the fully hadronic mode, which provides significantly larger statistics than leptonic decays. Hadronic VV decays can appear as resolved, semi boosted, or boosted topologies. The present analysis focuses on the boosted regime, where the decay of the two vector bosons shows up as two large-R jets, accompanied by two forward jets separated by a large rapidity gap. This topology is subject to an overwhelming background, namely from Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) multijet production, making signal extraction non-trivial.

To enhance sensitivity, this analysis uses several machine learning (ML) methods. Simple cut based selections often fail to capture correlations between jet kinematics, substructure and overall event topology that distinguishes VBS from background. The machine learning based selection is divided into two parts: the identification of the characteristic VBS topology and the construction of a machine learning based discriminant.
