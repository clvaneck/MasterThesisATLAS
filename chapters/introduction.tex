\markboth{INTRODUCTION}{Introduction}
The Standard Model (SM) is the most accurate framework describing the known elementary particles and their interactions~\cite{Thomson_ModernParticlePhysics_2013, Griffiths_IntroParticles_2008, ParticleDataGroup:2024cfk}. It is a quantum field theory, and the high-energy accelerator experiments that test it can probe interactions inaccessible elsewhere. The Standard Model is tested at CERN, where proton beams are brought into collision to study rare and energetic processes.

CERN operates a chain of accelerators that step by step increase the proton energy before injection into the Large Hadron Collider (LHC)~\cite{Evans:2008zzb}. The LHC currently delivers proton-proton collisions at a centre of mass energy of $13.6\,\mathrm{TeV}$ at four detector collision points. ATLAS~\cite{Aad:2008zzm} and CMS~\cite{CMS:2008zzk} are general purpose detectors designed to study a wide range of physics. LHCb~\cite{LHCb:2008vvz} focuses on heavy flavour dynamics, while ALICE~\cite{ALICE:2008ngc} studies the quark gluon plasma created in heavy ion collisions. The work presented in this thesis was done as part of the ATLAS collaboration.

This thesis concentrates on vector boson scattering (VBS)~\cite{ATLAS:ssWWobs:2019}, a rare electroweak process in which pairs of W or Z bosons are produced in association with two forward jets. The electroweak sector of the Standard Model predicts self interactions between gauge bosons through triple and quartic gauge couplings. Potential modifications of these gauge interactions, for example through anomalous quartic gauge couplings (aQGCs), can alter the scattering kinematics and lead to observable deviations from Standard Model predictions.

VBS can be studied in several decay channels. This thesis examines the fully hadronic mode, whose combined branching fraction exceeds that of the leptonic modes by roughly an order of magnitude~\cite{ParticleDataGroup:2024cfk}. Hadronic VV decays can appear as resolved, semi-boosted, or boosted topologies. This analysis focuses on the boosted regime, in which the decay of the two vector bosons appears as two large-R jets, accompanied by two forward jets separated by a large rapidity gap. The dominant background is Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) multijet production, which exceeds the signal cross section by approximately eight orders of magnitude and makes signal extraction challenging.

To enhance sensitivity, this analysis uses several machine learning (ML) methods. Simple cut-based selections often fail to capture correlations between jet kinematics, substructure and overall event topology that distinguish VBS from background. The machine-learning-based selection is divided into two parts: the identification of the characteristic VBS topology and the construction of a machine-learning-based discriminant.
