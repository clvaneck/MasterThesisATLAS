\section{The Standard Model}

The Standard Model of particle physics is a quantum field theory describing the known
elementary particles and three of the four fundamental interactions
\cite{Thomson_ModernParticlePhysics_2013, Griffiths_IntroParticles_2008}.  It is
formulated as a Yang--Mills gauge theory \cite{YangMills_Symmetry_1954} with local
symmetry group
\begin{equation}
SU(3)_{C}\times SU(2)_{L}\times U(1)_{Y},
\end{equation}
which fixes the form of all interaction terms.  The dynamics of the gauge fields are
encoded in the field-strength tensors
\begin{align}
G_{\mu\nu}^{a}
  &= \partial_{\mu}G_{\nu}^{a} - \partial_{\nu}G_{\mu}^{a}
     + g_{s}\,f^{abc}G_{\mu}^{b}G_{\nu}^{c}, \\
W_{\mu\nu}^{a}
  &= \partial_{\mu}W_{\nu}^{a} - \partial_{\nu}W_{\mu}^{a}
     + g\,\epsilon^{abc}W_{\mu}^{b}W_{\nu}^{c}, \\
B_{\mu\nu}
  &= \partial_{\mu}B_{\nu} - \partial_{\nu}B_{\mu},
\end{align}
for $SU(3)_{C}$, $SU(2)_{L}$, and $U(1)_{Y}$ respectively, where $f^{abc}$ and
$\epsilon^{abc}$ are the relevant structure constants.  The non-Abelian terms in
$G_{\mu\nu}^{a}$ and $W_{\mu\nu}^{a}$ give rise to cubic and quartic self-interactions
among the gluons and weak bosons, which are the origin of the triple and quartic gauge
couplings central to this thesis.  The purely kinetic part of the gauge sector is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{gauge}}
= -\tfrac{1}{4}G_{\mu\nu}^{a}G^{a\,\mu\nu}
  -\tfrac{1}{4}W_{\mu\nu}^{a}W^{a\,\mu\nu}
  -\tfrac{1}{4}B_{\mu\nu}B^{\mu\nu}.
\end{equation}

Matter fields couple to the gauge sector through the covariant derivative.  For a field
transforming under all three factors the derivative is
\begin{equation}
D_{\mu}
= \partial_{\mu}
  + ig_{s}T^{a}G_{\mu}^{a}
  + ig\,\tfrac{\tau^{a}}{2}W_{\mu}^{a}
  + ig'\,\tfrac{Y}{2}B_{\mu},
\label{eq:covderiv}
\end{equation}
where $T^{a}$, $\tau^{a}/2$, and $Y/2$ are the generators of $SU(3)_{C}$,
$SU(2)_{L}$, and $U(1)_{Y}$.  Replacing $\partial_{\mu}\to D_{\mu}$ in the kinetic
terms of the fermion and Higgs Lagrangians generates all gauge interactions in a
form dictated entirely by the group structure.

The particle content is shown in fig.~\ref{fig:SM}.  Quarks are colour triplets and
$SU(2)_{L}$ doublets for left-handed chirality but singlets for right-handed
chirality, reflecting the chiral nature of the weak force.  Leptons are colour
singlets.  Fermion masses are forbidden by gauge invariance as bare mass terms and
arise instead from Yukawa couplings after electroweak symmetry breaking
\cite{Higgs_BEH_A_1964, Higgs_BEH_B_1964, EnglertBrout_BEH_1964}.

\section{Electroweak symmetry breaking}

The Higgs field is a complex $SU(2)_{L}$ doublet of hypercharge $Y=1$,
\begin{equation}
\Phi = \begin{pmatrix}\phi^{+}\\\phi^{0}\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
with scalar potential $V(\Phi)=\mu^{2}\Phi^{\dagger}\Phi+\lambda(\Phi^{\dagger}\Phi)^{2}$.
For $\mu^{2}<0$ the potential has a ring of degenerate minima at
$|\Phi|^{2}=v^{2}/2$ with $v=\sqrt{-\mu^{2}/\lambda}\approx 246\ \mathrm{GeV}$.
Expanding around the minimum in unitary gauge,
$\Phi = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\bigl(0,\ v+h\bigr)^{T}$,
the Higgs kinetic term $(D_{\mu}\Phi)^{\dagger}(D^{\mu}\Phi)$ generates mass terms for the
gauge bosons,
\begin{equation}
M_{W} = \tfrac{1}{2}gv, \qquad
M_{Z} = \tfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{g^{2}+g'^{2}}\,v, \qquad
M_{\gamma} = 0,
\end{equation}
while the physical photon $A_{\mu}$ and $Z_{\mu}$ arise as the orthogonal mixtures
$A_{\mu}=\cos\theta_{W}B_{\mu}+\sin\theta_{W}W_{\mu}^{3}$ and
$Z_{\mu}=-\sin\theta_{W}B_{\mu}+\cos\theta_{W}W_{\mu}^{3}$, with the weak mixing angle
$\tan\theta_{W}=g'/g$.  Crucially, the same kinetic term also fixes the
$HVV$ and $HHVV$ couplings, and via the non-Abelian structure of $W_{\mu\nu}^{a}$,
the triple gauge couplings $WWZ/WW\gamma$ and quartic gauge couplings $WWWW$,
$WWZZ$, $WWZ\gamma$, $WW\gamma\gamma$.  Any modification to these interactions relative
to their SM values constitutes an anomalous gauge coupling.

illustrated in fig.~\ref{fig:vbs}, in which two incoming quarks radiate weak bosons that

from the five gauge-invariant topologies shown in fig.~\ref{fig:vbs_topologies}: a
quartic gauge contact interaction, $t/u$- and $s$-channel exchanges built from two

The sensitivity of VBS to new physics is most clearly seen through the lens of
unitarity.  The longitudinal polarisation vector of a massive gauge boson with
four-momentum $p^{\mu}=(E,\mathbf{p})$ is
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon_{L}^{\mu}
= \frac{1}{M_{V}}\!\left(|\mathbf{p}|,\, E\hat{\mathbf{p}}\right)
\xrightarrow{E\gg M_{V}} \frac{p^{\mu}}{M_{V}} + \mathcal{O}\!\left(\frac{M_{V}}{E}\right).
\end{equation}
By the Goldstone boson equivalence theorem, scattering of longitudinally polarised
bosons at high energy is equivalent to scattering of the Goldstone components of
$\Phi$ \cite{LlewellynSmith_Unitarity_1973, Cornwall_Unitarity_1974}.  Without the
Higgs diagrams the partial-wave amplitude for $W_{L}^{+}W_{L}^{-}\to W_{L}^{+}W_{L}^{-}$
grows as $g^{2}\hat{s}/M_{W}^{2}$, violating the unitarity bound at
$\sqrt{\hat{s}}\sim 1\ \mathrm{TeV}$.  Higgs exchange cancels this growth exactly,
leaving an amplitude that falls with energy.  Any modification of the quartic gauge
vertex or the $HVV$ coupling disrupts this cancellation and restores power-law growth,
making VBS an exceptionally sensitive probe of the electroweak sector.

In the fully hadronic channel both vector bosons decay as $V\to q\bar{q}$, yielding
the highest expected event rate because the combined hadronic branching fractions
$\mathcal{B}(W\to qq')\approx 67\%$ and $\mathcal{B}(Z\to qq)\approx 70\%$ greatly exceed
leptonic modes.  The experimental challenge is the large QCD multijet background.

\section{Beyond the Standard Model}

The SM does not incorporate gravity, cannot explain the observed dark matter and dark
energy content of the universe \cite{Riess_DE_1998, RubinFordThonnard_DM_1980}, does
not account for the baryon asymmetry \cite{Gavela_CP_1994}, and must be extended to
accommodate neutrino masses \cite{Fukuda_Nu_1998, Ahmad_SNO_2001}.  These shortcomings
motivate the hypothesis that the SM is the low-energy limit of a more complete theory
with a characteristic scale $\Lambda$ beyond direct experimental reach.  Because such
new states may be too heavy to produce directly, precision measurements of processes
sensitive to the gauge structure of electroweak symmetry breaking offer an indirect
route.  VBS is particularly well suited: any heavy sector that couples to the gauge or
Higgs fields will leave imprints in the quartic and triple gauge vertices at low energy,
distorting cross sections, angular distributions, and polarisation fractions in ways
that can be systematically parameterised by an effective field theory.

\section{Effective Field Theory}

If new physics resides at a scale $\Lambda \gg E$, its low-energy effects can be
captured by integrating out the heavy fields and organising the resulting corrections
as a tower of local operators suppressed by powers of $\Lambda$
\cite{AppelquistCarazzone_EFT_1975}.  The prototypical example is the Fermi theory of
weak decays \cite{Fermi_Beta_1933}: the four-fermion operator
$G_{F}(\bar{\psi}\gamma^{\mu}\psi)^{2}$ encodes W-boson exchange at energies $E\ll M_W$
with coupling $G_{F}/\sqrt{2}=g^{2}/(8M_{W}^{2})$, and it remained predictive for
decades before the W was directly discovered.

\subsection{Standard Model Effective Field Theory}

The Standard Model Effective Field Theory (SMEFT) \cite{BrivioTrott_EFT_2019} extends
the SM Lagrangian by the complete set of higher-dimensional operators that are
gauge-invariant under $SU(3)_{C}\times SU(2)_{L}\times U(1)_{Y}$ and built from SM
fields.  With baryon and lepton number conserved, the leading corrections appear at
dimension six,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{SMEFT}}
=
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{SM}}
+ \sum_{i}\frac{c_{i}}{\Lambda^{2}}\,\mathcal{O}^{(6)}_{i}
+ \sum_{j}\frac{d_{j}}{\Lambda^{4}}\,\mathcal{O}^{(8)}_{j}
+ \cdots
\label{eq:smeft}
\end{equation}
Dimension-6 operators can modify triple gauge couplings and, after EWSB, induce
quartic gauge structures that are rigidly correlated with those triple-coupling
shifts.  Operators that generate genuinely independent quartic gauge interactions,
uncorrelated with any triple gauge modification, first appear at dimension eight.
Searches for aQGCs therefore probe the dimension-8 coefficients $d_j/\Lambda^4$
directly.
\newpage

The physical consequence of a nonzero coefficient is most transparent at the amplitude
level.  For a process with a single dimension-8 insertion,
$\mathcal{M}=\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{SM}}+d\,\mathcal{M}_{8}$, the cross section receives
\begin{equation}
|\mathcal{M}|^{2}
=
|\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{SM}}|^{2}
+ \underbrace{2d\,\Re\!\left(\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{SM}}^{*}\mathcal{M}_{8}\right)}_{\text{linear}}
+ \underbrace{d^{2}|\mathcal{M}_{8}|^{2}}_{\text{quadratic (QUAD)}},
\end{equation}
a linear interference term (INT) that dominates near the SM prediction and a purely
quadratic term (QUAD) that becomes important when the EFT contribution is large.  Because
$\mathcal{M}_{8}\propto E^{4}/\Lambda^{4}$, both terms grow steeply with energy,
concentrating the sensitivity of an aQGC search in the high-$p_{T}$ tails of VBS
distributions.

\section{Anomalous Quartic Gauge Couplings}

In the SM the quartic gauge vertices arise exclusively from the
$(D_\mu\Phi)^\dagger(D^\mu\Phi)$ term and from the $W_{\mu\nu}^aW^{a\,\mu\nu}$
kinetic term, and their strengths are completely fixed by $g$, $g'$, and $M_V$.
Dimension-8 operators introduce new $VVVV$ contact structures not generated by these
sources, producing anomalous quartic gauge couplings (aQGCs).  The amplitude
enhancement
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{M}_{8}\propto \frac{E^{4}}{\Lambda^{4}}
\end{equation}
makes VBS at large invariant mass the primary experimental handle.  The independent
operators are classified by field content into three families
\cite{Eboli_2016}: scalar-type ($\mathcal{O}_{S}$), built from Higgs
covariant derivatives only; mixed-type ($\mathcal{O}_{M}$), coupling field-strength
tensors to Higgs covariant derivatives; and tensor-type ($\mathcal{O}_{T}$), built
from field-strength tensors only.  The three families couple to distinct combinations
of gauge boson polarisation modes, as summarised in table~\ref{tab:aqgc_families},
which has direct consequences for how strongly each family interferes with the SM
amplitude.  Because the SM quartic vertex is dominated by longitudinal scattering
at high energy, operators that excite longitudinal modes share helicity configurations
with the SM and produce a large linear interference term
$2d\,\Re(\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{SM}}^{*}\mathcal{M}_{8})$.  Operators that couple only
to transverse modes are orthogonal to the leading SM helicity structure and their
contribution to the cross section is dominated by the quadratic term
$d^{2}|\mathcal{M}_{8}|^{2}$.

\subsection{Scalar-type operators}
Scalar-type operators are formed from four covariant derivatives of the Higgs doublet
$\Phi$ with no field-strength tensors.  The covariant derivative acting on the
$SU(2)_L$ doublet is
\begin{equation}
D_{\mu}\Phi
= \left(\partial_{\mu}
  + \tfrac{i}{2}g\,\tau^{a}W_{\mu}^{a}
  + \tfrac{i}{2}g'B_{\mu}\right)\Phi,
\end{equation}
and the two independent dimension-8 operators in this class are \cite{Eboli_2016}
\begin{align}
\mathcal{O}_{S,0}
&= \left[(D_{\mu}\Phi)^{\dagger}(D_{\nu}\Phi)\right]
   \left[(D^{\mu}\Phi)^{\dagger}(D^{\nu}\Phi)\right], \\[4pt]
\mathcal{O}_{S,1}
&= \left[(D_{\mu}\Phi)^{\dagger}(D^{\mu}\Phi)\right]
   \left[(D_{\nu}\Phi)^{\dagger}(D^{\nu}\Phi)\right].
\end{align}
After EWSB the Goldstone boson equivalence theorem identifies $D_\mu\Phi$ with the
longitudinal polarisation vector at high energy, so these operators generate contact
interactions exclusively among longitudinally polarised bosons.  The operator
$\mathcal{O}_{S,0}$ mixes the Lorentz indices of the two bilinears, producing helicity
structures distinct from $\mathcal{O}_{S,1}$, which contracts each bilinear
independently.  The scalar-sector Lagrangian is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{S}
= \frac{f_{S,0}}{\Lambda^{4}}\,\mathcal{O}_{S,0}
+ \frac{f_{S,1}}{\Lambda^{4}}\,\mathcal{O}_{S,1}.
\end{equation}
Because the SM quartic amplitude is itself largest in the longitudinal channel, scalar
operators share the same helicity configurations as the SM and produce a large linear
interference term.  Consequently, even small values of $f_{S,i}/\Lambda^4$ lead to
observable deviations in the total cross section, and the linear term dominates over
the quadratic BSM contribution across most of the experimentally accessible energy
range.  Unitarity constraints on the Wilson coefficients $f_{S,i}$ are studied in
\cite{PhysRevD.101.113003}.

\subsection{Mixed-type operators}

Mixed-type operators contain one or two gauge field-strength tensors alongside two
Higgs covariant derivatives.  The relevant field-strength objects are
\begin{equation}
\hat{W}_{\mu\nu}
= \tfrac{i}{2}g\,\tau^{a}W_{\mu\nu}^{a}, \qquad
B_{\mu\nu} = \partial_{\mu}B_{\nu}-\partial_{\nu}B_{\mu}.
\end{equation}
The seven independent operators are \cite{Eboli_2016}
\begin{align}
\mathcal{O}_{M,0}
&= \mathrm{Tr}\!\left[\hat{W}_{\mu\nu}\hat{W}^{\mu\nu}\right]
   \left[(D_{\beta}\Phi)^{\dagger}(D^{\beta}\Phi)\right], \\[3pt]
\mathcal{O}_{M,1}
&= \mathrm{Tr}\!\left[\hat{W}_{\mu\nu}\hat{W}^{\nu\beta}\right]
   \left[(D_{\beta}\Phi)^{\dagger}(D^{\mu}\Phi)\right], \\[3pt]
\mathcal{O}_{M,2}
&= B_{\mu\nu}B^{\mu\nu}
   \left[(D_{\beta}\Phi)^{\dagger}(D^{\beta}\Phi)\right], \\[3pt]
\mathcal{O}_{M,3}
&= B_{\mu\nu}B^{\nu\beta}
   \left[(D_{\beta}\Phi)^{\dagger}(D^{\mu}\Phi)\right], \\[3pt]
\mathcal{O}_{M,4}
&= \left[(D_{\mu}\Phi)^{\dagger}\hat{W}_{\beta\nu}(D^{\mu}\Phi)\right]B^{\beta\nu}, \\[3pt]
\mathcal{O}_{M,5}
&= \left[(D_{\mu}\Phi)^{\dagger}\hat{W}_{\beta\nu}(D^{\nu}\Phi)\right]B^{\beta\mu}
   +\mathrm{h.c.}, \\[3pt]
\mathcal{O}_{M,7}
&= \left[(D_{\mu}\Phi)^{\dagger}\hat{W}_{\beta\nu}\hat{W}^{\beta\mu}(D^{\nu}\Phi)\right]
   +\mathrm{h.c.}
\end{align}
The operator $\mathcal{O}_{M,6}$ reduces to a combination of lower-dimension operators
after integration by parts and use of the equations of motion, and is omitted from the
independent basis \cite{Eboli_2016}.  The mixed-sector Lagrangian is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{M}
= \sum_{\substack{i=0\\i\neq 6}}^{7}
  \frac{f_{M,i}}{\Lambda^{4}}\,\mathcal{O}_{M,i}.
\end{equation}
Because each operator contains both field-strength tensors and Higgs covariant
derivatives, mixed-type operators excite both transverse and longitudinal polarisation
modes.  The longitudinal component overlaps with the SM amplitude and contributes a
linear interference term, while the transverse component contributes primarily through
the quadratic term.  The net interference with the SM is therefore intermediate between
the scalar and tensor families: larger than for tensor operators, but reduced relative
to the scalar case by the dilution from transverse helicity configurations that do not
overlap with the dominant SM amplitude.  Unitarity bounds on the $f_{M,i}$ are derived
in \cite{PhysRevD.101.113003}.

\subsection{Tensor-type operators}

Tensor-type operators are built entirely from gauge field-strength tensors with no
Higgs sector content.  Using the shorthand
$\hat{W}_{\mu\nu}\equiv\tfrac{i}{2}g\tau^a W_{\mu\nu}^a$,
the ten operators are \cite{Eboli_2016}
\begin{align}
\mathcal{O}_{T,0}
&= \mathrm{Tr}\!\left[\hat{W}_{\mu\nu}\hat{W}^{\mu\nu}\right]
   \mathrm{Tr}\!\left[\hat{W}_{\alpha\beta}\hat{W}^{\alpha\beta}\right], \\[3pt]
\mathcal{O}_{T,1}
&= \mathrm{Tr}\!\left[\hat{W}_{\alpha\nu}\hat{W}^{\mu\beta}\right]
   \mathrm{Tr}\!\left[\hat{W}_{\mu\beta}\hat{W}^{\alpha\nu}\right], \\[3pt]
\mathcal{O}_{T,2}
&= \mathrm{Tr}\!\left[\hat{W}_{\alpha\mu}\hat{W}^{\mu\beta}\right]
   \mathrm{Tr}\!\left[\hat{W}_{\beta\nu}\hat{W}^{\nu\alpha}\right], \\[3pt]
\mathcal{O}_{T,3}
&= \mathrm{Tr}\!\left[\hat{W}_{\mu\nu}\hat{W}^{\mu\nu}\hat{W}_{\alpha\beta}\hat{W}^{\alpha\beta}\right], \\[3pt]
\mathcal{O}_{T,4}
&= \mathrm{Tr}\!\left[\hat{W}_{\alpha\mu}\hat{W}^{\mu\beta}\hat{W}_{\beta\nu}\hat{W}^{\nu\alpha}\right], \\[3pt]
\mathcal{O}_{T,5}
&= \mathrm{Tr}\!\left[\hat{W}_{\mu\nu}\hat{W}^{\mu\nu}\right]
   B_{\alpha\beta}B^{\alpha\beta}, \\[3pt]
\mathcal{O}_{T,6}
&= \mathrm{Tr}\!\left[\hat{W}_{\alpha\nu}\hat{W}^{\mu\beta}\right]
   B_{\mu\beta}B^{\alpha\nu}, \\[3pt]
\mathcal{O}_{T,7}
&= \mathrm{Tr}\!\left[\hat{W}_{\alpha\mu}\hat{W}^{\mu\beta}\right]
   B_{\beta\nu}B^{\nu\alpha}, \\[3pt]
\mathcal{O}_{T,8}
&= B_{\mu\nu}B^{\mu\nu}B_{\alpha\beta}B^{\alpha\beta}, \\[3pt]
\mathcal{O}_{T,9}
&= B_{\alpha\mu}B^{\mu\beta}B_{\beta\nu}B^{\nu\alpha}.
\end{align}
Some bases use the Cayley--Hamilton identity for $SU(2)$ matrices to rewrite
$\mathcal{O}_{T,3}$ and $\mathcal{O}_{T,4}$ in terms of $\mathcal{O}_{T,0}$,
$\mathcal{O}_{T,1}$, and $\mathcal{O}_{T,2}$.  Here they are kept explicitly to
track all $T_{0\ldots 9}$ coefficients.  The tensor-sector Lagrangian is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{T}
= \sum_{i=0}^{9}
   \frac{f_{T,i}}{\Lambda^{4}}\,\mathcal{O}_{T,i}.
\end{equation}
The absence of Higgs derivatives means these operators couple exclusively to
transversely polarised gauge bosons.  Since the SM quartic amplitude is dominated
by longitudinal helicity configurations at high energy, tensor operators are
essentially orthogonal to the SM in helicity space, and the linear interference
term $2d\,\Re(\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{SM}}^{*}\mathcal{M}_{T})$ is strongly suppressed.
Their contribution to the cross section is therefore dominated by the purely quadratic
term $d^{2}|\mathcal{M}_{T}|^{2}$, which means tensor operators only become visible
at large $f_{T,i}/\Lambda^4$ or at very high energies where the quadratic term
overtakes the SM.  Despite this suppressed interference, the four-field-strength
structure produces the steepest energy growth
$\mathcal{M}_T\propto g^4\hat{s}^2/\Lambda^4$ among the three families.
Operators $\mathcal{O}_{T,0}$--$\mathcal{O}_{T,2}$ involve only $SU(2)_L$ field
strengths and affect $WW$, $WZ$, and $ZZ$ channels, while the mixed
$\hat{W}^2B^2$ operators $\mathcal{O}_{T,5}$--$\mathcal{O}_{T,7}$ additionally
enter processes involving a hypercharge field-strength insertion, and the purely
$U(1)_Y$ operators $\mathcal{O}_{T,8}$ and $\mathcal{O}_{T,9}$ contribute primarily
to photon- and $Z$-initiated channels.  Unitarity constraints on the $f_{T,i}$ are
derived in \cite{PhysRevD.101.113003}.
