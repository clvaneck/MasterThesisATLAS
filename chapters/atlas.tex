\chapter{The ATLAS experiment}
\label{chap:atlas}

The A Toroidal LHC ApparatuS (ATLAS) experiment~\cite{Aad:2008zzm} is the largest general purpose detector at the LHC. It combines tracking, calorimetry and muon detection in concentric cylindrical layers around the beam axis (Figure~\ref{fig:atlas_detector}), measuring charged-particle trajectories, particle energies and event-level observables across a broad range of final-state topologies. The coordinate system originates at the nominal interaction point, with the $z$-axis along the beam direction, the $x$-axis toward the centre of the LHC ring and the $y$-axis upward. Particle directions are described by the azimuthal angle $\phi$ and the pseudorapidity $\eta = -\ln\tan(\theta/2)$, where $\theta$ is the polar angle from the beam axis. Angular separations are quantified by $\Delta R = \sqrt{(\Delta\eta)^2 + (\Delta\phi)^2}$, a quantity central to jet reconstruction.

\section{Large Hadron Collider}
The Large Hadron Collider (LHC)~\cite{Evans:2008zzb} is a 27~km circular proton-proton accelerator near Geneva, where superconducting dipole magnets producing fields of 8.3~T guide two counter-rotating beams to collide at several interaction points, including ATLAS. During Run~2 (2015--2018) about 140~fb$^{-1}$ at $\sqrt{s} = 13$~TeV of collision data was recorded. This analysis uses the Run~2 dataset and corresponding simulation, with Run~3 data to be added in the future.

\section{The ATLAS detector}
Moving outward from the interaction point, the inner detector reconstructs charged-particle trajectories within $|\eta| < 2.5$ inside a 2~T solenoidal field aligned with the beam axis. The curvature of charged tracks gives access to the transverse momentum via $r = p_\mathrm{T}/(qB)$. Its silicon pixel and Semiconductor Tracker (SCT) layers, complemented by the Transition Radiation Tracker (TRT), sit close to the interaction point and enable vertex reconstruction and the discrimination between tracks from the primary hard scatter and from additional pile-up interactions.

Surrounding the inner detector, the calorimeter system measures particle energies by absorbing them and sampling the resulting electromagnetic and hadronic showers, with coverage extending to $|\eta| < 4.9$. The electromagnetic calorimeter, optimised for electrons and photons, provides a cell granularity of $\Delta\eta \approx 0.025$. Strongly interacting particles are absorbed in the hadronic calorimeter, which has a coarser granularity of $\Delta\eta \approx 0.1$. Energy deposits in both systems are combined into topological clusters that serve as inputs for object reconstruction.

The outermost subsystem is the muon spectrometer. Muons traverse the calorimeters with little energy loss and are measured at large radius from the curvature of their trajectories in toroidal magnetic fields. Combining this with the inner-detector track yields the final muon momentum.

Because full readout and storage at the nominal 40~MHz bunch-crossing rate is infeasible, ATLAS uses a two-level trigger to select events of interest in real time. The hardware-based Level-1 trigger identifies Regions of Interest from energy and momentum thresholds and reduces the rate to approximately 100~kHz, after which the software-based High Level Trigger performs refined reconstruction on the full detector response to reach roughly 1~kHz for permanent storage. Offline, the subsystem information is combined into the physics objects used in analysis: tracks, energy clusters, jets and missing transverse momentum.

\section{Jet reconstruction}
Quarks and gluons produced in the hard scattering process cannot be observed directly: they undergo parton showering and hadronisation into collimated sprays of hadrons, which show up in the detector as localised patterns of energy deposits and tracks reconstructed as jets. Jets therefore serve as experimental proxies for the underlying hard interaction and subsequent QCD dynamics.

Jets are reconstructed by clustering lower-level objects using sequential recombination algorithms. The anti-$k_t$ algorithm~\cite{Cacciari:2008gp}, the ATLAS standard, defines two distance measures,
\begin{equation}
    d_{ij} = \min\!\left(p_{\mathrm{T},i}^{-2},\, p_{\mathrm{T},j}^{-2}\right) \frac{\Delta R_{ij}^2}{R^2}, \qquad d_{iB} = p_{\mathrm{T},i}^{-2},
\end{equation}
where $\Delta R_{ij}^2 = (\eta_i - \eta_j)^2 + (\phi_i - \phi_j)^2$ is the squared angular distance between objects $i$ and $j$ and $R$ is the jet radius parameter. At each step the smallest of all $d_{ij}$ and $d_{iB}$ is identified: if a $d_{ij}$ is smallest, objects $i$ and $j$ are merged. If a $d_{iB}$ is smallest, object $i$ is removed and called a jet. The $p_{\mathrm{T}}^{-2}$ weighting causes hard particles to cluster their soft neighbours first, yielding approximately conical jets that are stable against soft and collinear radiation.

Jet constituents, the reconstructed objects that serve as inputs to the clustering algorithm, are constructed in two complementary ways. Particle Flow (PFlow) objects~\cite{ATLAS:PFlow:2017} combine inner-detector tracks with calorimeter energy clusters, exploiting the superior tracker momentum resolution at low $p_\mathrm{T}$ to improve the jet energy resolution. Unified Flow Objects (UFO)~\cite{ATLAS:UFO:2021} extend this to the large-$R$ regime by combining PFlow objects with topological clusters, giving sensitivity to both charged and neutral particle content within boosted jet substructure.

Two jet-size regimes are used in this analysis. Small-$R$ jets ($R = 0.4$, PFlow constituents) reconstruct the showering of individual quarks and gluons, while large-$R$ jets ($R = 1.0$, UFO constituents) capture the full hadronic decay of a boosted heavy particle, such as a $W$ or $Z$ boson, within a single jet cone.
