\chapter{The ATLAS experiment}
\label{chap:atlas}

\section{Overview}
The A Toroidal LHC ApparatuS (ATLAS) experiment~\cite{Aad:2008zzm} is the largest general purpose detector at the LHC. It combines tracking, calorimetry and muon detection in concentric cylindrical layers around the beam axis, providing precise measurements of charged particle trajectories, particle energies and event-level observables across a broad range of final-state topologies, as illustrated in figure~\ref{fig:atlas_detector}. The coordinate system has its origin at the nominal interaction point, with the $z$-axis along the beam direction, the $x$-axis pointing toward the centre of the LHC ring and the $y$-axis pointing upward. Particle directions are described by the azimuthal angle $\phi$ and the pseudorapidity $\eta = -\ln\tan(\theta/2)$, where $\theta$ is the polar angle from the beam axis. Angular separations are quantified by $\Delta R = \sqrt{(\Delta\eta)^2 + (\Delta\phi)^2}$, a quantity central to jet reconstruction.

\section{Large Hadron Collider}
The Large Hadron Collider (LHC)~\cite{Evans:2008zzb} is a 27~km circular proton-proton accelerator near Geneva, where superconducting dipole magnets producing fields of 8.3~T guide two counter-rotating beams to collide at several interaction points, including ATLAS. Each beam consists of bunches of order $10^{11}$ protons separated by 25~ns. Run~1 (2010--2012) delivered approximately 25~fb$^{-1}$ at $\sqrt{s} = 7$ and 8~TeV, while Run~2 (2015--2018) provided about 140~fb$^{-1}$ at $\sqrt{s} = 13$~TeV, this analysis uses Run~2 data and corresponding simulated events.

\section{Inner detector}
The inner detector reconstructs charged-particle trajectories within $|\eta| < 2.5$ and operates inside a 2~T solenoidal field aligned with the beam axis. The resulting curvature of charged tracks gives access to the transverse momentum via $r = p_\mathrm{T}/(qB)$, so that $p_\mathrm{T}$ is inversely proportional to the track curvature $\kappa = 1/r$. Three subsystems contribute: the silicon pixel detector at the innermost radii , the surrounding Semiconductor Tracker (SCT) and the Transition Radiation Tracker (TRT). The proximity of these layers to the interaction point enables vertex reconstruction and discrimination between tracks from the primary hard scatter and those from additional pile-up interactions.

The current inner detector is being replaced by the Inner Tracker (ITk)~\cite{ATLAS:ITkPixelTDR} for the High Luminosity LHC (HL-LHC), which will operate at higher instantaneous luminosities and pile-up. The ITk is an all-silicon detector of pixel and strip layers. During this thesis, I contributed to the testing setup for the ITk cooling capillary system, which is critical for maintaining stable silicon-sensor operating temperatures under the increased load of the HL-LHC.

\section{Calorimeter system}
Surrounding the inner detector, the calorimeter system measures particle energies by absorbing them and sampling the resulting electromagnetic and hadronic showers, with coverage extending to $|\eta| < 4.9$. The electromagnetic calorimeter is optimised for electrons and photons, which deposit their energy through bremsstrahlung and pair production and provides a pseudorapidity resolution of $\Delta\eta \approx 0.025$. Strongly interacting particles typically traverse the electromagnetic calorimeter with only partial energy loss and are absorbed in the hadronic calorimeter, which has a coarser resolution of $\Delta\eta \approx 0.1$ due to the larger fluctuations of hadronic showers. Energy deposits in both systems are combined into localised topological clusters that serve as inputs for higher-level object reconstruction.
\section{Muon spectrometer}
The outermost subsystem is the muon spectrometer. Muons traverse the calorimeters with little energy loss and are measured at large radius from the curvature of their trajectories in large toroidal magnetic fields, independently of the inner detector. The combination with inner detector tracks yields the muon momentum reconstruction. 
\section{Trigger and event reconstruction}
At the nominal LHC bunch-crossing rate of 40~MHz, full readout and storage of every event is infeasible and ATLAS uses a two-level trigger system to select events of interest in real time. The hardware-based Level-1 (L1) trigger identifies Regions of Interest from energy and momentum thresholds and reduces the rate from 40~MHz to approximately 100~kHz, after which the software-based High Level Trigger (HLT) performs refined reconstruction on the full detector response and reduces the rate to roughly 1~kHz for permanent storage. Offline event reconstruction then combines information from the inner detector, calorimeters and muon spectrometer into physics objects, including charged-particle tracks, energy clusters, jets and missing transverse momentum, which form the basis for analysis.

\section{Jet reconstruction}
Quarks and gluons produced in the hard scattering process cannot be observed directly, instead, they undergo parton showering and hadronisation, resulting in collimated sprays of hadrons. In the detector, these sprays manifest as localised patterns of energy deposits and tracks, which are reconstructed as jets. Jets therefore serve as experimental proxies for the underlying hard interaction and subsequent QCD dynamics.

Jets are reconstructed by clustering lower level objects using sequential recombination algorithms. The anti-$k_t$ algorithm~\cite{Cacciari:2008gp} is the standard jet clustering algorithm used in ATLAS. It defines two distance measures,
\begin{equation}
    d_{ij} = \min\!\left(p_{\mathrm{T},i}^{-2},\, p_{\mathrm{T},j}^{-2}\right) \frac{\Delta R_{ij}^2}{R^2}, \qquad d_{iB} = p_{\mathrm{T},i}^{-2},
\end{equation}
where $\Delta R_{ij}^2 = (\eta_i - \eta_j)^2 + (\phi_i - \phi_j)^2$ is the squared angular distance between objects $i$ and $j$ and $R$ is the jet radius parameter. At each step, the algorithm identifies the smallest of all $d_{ij}$ and $d_{iB}$. If a $d_{ij}$ is smallest, objects $i$ and $j$ are merged, if $d_{iB}$ is smallest, object $i$ is removed from the list and called a jet. The use of $p_{\mathrm{T}}^{-2}$ weights causes hard particles to cluster their soft neighbours before soft particles cluster amongst themselves, resulting in approximately conical jets with well-defined boundaries that are stable against soft and collinear radiation.

Jet constituents are the reconstructed objects that serve as inputs to the clustering algorithm. ATLAS uses two complementary approaches to construct these inputs. Particle Flow (PFlow) objects are formed by combining inner detector tracks with calorimeter energy clusters, exploiting the superior momentum resolution of the tracker at low $p_\mathrm{T}$ to improve the overall jet energy resolution. Unified Flow Objects (UFO) extend this concept to the large-$R$ jet regime by combining PFlow objects with topological clusters, enabling sensitivity to both charged and neutral particle content within boosted jet substructure.

Two jet size regimes are used in this analysis. Small-$R$ jets are reconstructed with radius parameter $R = 0.4$ using PFlow constituents. They are well suited for reconstructing the showering of individual quarks and gluons. Large-$R$ jets are reconstructed with $R = 1.0$ using UFO constituents and are designed to capture the full hadronic decay of a boosted heavy particle, such as a $W$ or $Z$ boson, within a single jet cone.

The set of constituents associated with a jet provides detailed information about its internal structure. Quantities such as the number of constituents, their transverse momentum distribution and their angular correlations are sensitive to the underlying physical process and form the basis for jet substructure tagging techniques.
