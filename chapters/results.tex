\chapter{Performance of a VBS topology identifier}
\label{chap:vbfrnn_results}

This chapter evaluates the VBF-RNN tagger introduced in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn} on its ability to isolate the vector boson scattering topology from the inclusive electroweak $VVjj$ production. The three RNN versions of Table~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_versions} are first compared on a common gauge of performance, after which the artefacts introduced by the jet multiplicity are discussed and the response of the tagger to the different aQGC operator families is studied, finally an alternative transformer-based architecture is evaluated. The performance reported here motivates the choice of the version~3 score as both a pre-selection requirement and an input feature to the aQGC discriminant of Chapter~\ref{chap:analysismethods}.

\section{Event selection and evaluation}
\label{sec:vbfrnn_eval}

The tagger is evaluated on the statistically independent validation sample of electroweak $VVjj$ events described in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn}, using the truth-level VBS / non-VBS labels obtained from the boson- and tagging-jet matching procedure. The combined $WW$, $WZ$ and $ZZ$ final states are merged into a single sample, so that the quoted performance reflects the inclusive diboson channel relevant to the fully hadronic analysis, the channel-by-channel breakdown is given in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn_perf}.

Because the tagger output is a continuous score $s\in[0,1]$, its discriminating power is quantified with the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve. For a given threshold $s_{\mathrm{cut}}$, events with $s>s_{\mathrm{cut}}$ are tagged as VBS. The true-positive rate (the VBS efficiency, or VBS positive rate) is the fraction of true VBS events that are tagged, while the false-positive rate (the non-VBS positive rate) is the fraction of non-VBS EWK events that are also tagged. Sweeping $s_{\mathrm{cut}}$ from $1$ to $0$ traces out the ROC curve in the (false-positive rate, true-positive rate) plane. A perfect classifier reaches the top-left corner, whereas a random classifier lies on the diagonal. The area under this curve (AUC) condenses the curve into a single scalar: it equals the probability that a randomly chosen VBS event is assigned a higher score than a randomly chosen non-VBS event, so that $\mathrm{AUC}=1$ is perfect separation and $\mathrm{AUC}=0.5$ is no separation. The AUC is used throughout this chapter as the primary gauge of performance.

Although the AUC provides a convenient scalar summary, it weights all score thresholds equally, whereas the tagger is not used as a hard classifier in the analysis. The score enters both as a loose pre-selection requirement and as a continuous input feature to the aQGC discriminant of Chapter~\ref{chap:analysismethods}. The operationally relevant region of the ROC curve is therefore the high-VBS-efficiency, low-threshold regime, where the signal is retained almost in full while the non-VBS EWK component is only mildly suppressed, and the full shape of the score distribution, not the AUC alone, is what ultimately propagates into the discriminant. Both the AUC and the score distributions are consequently reported in what follows.

\section{Comparison of the tagger versions}
\label{sec:vbfrnn_perf}

The three VBF-RNN versions of Table~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_versions} differ both in their training target and in the set of input jets, and the comparison below is constructed to disentangle the impact of each. Version~1 was trained on a different objective, resonant VBF against gluon-fusion production, so the decision boundary it learned reflects that problem rather than the VBS versus non-VBS EWK separation required here. When the negative class changes from gluon-fusion jets to the other electroweak $VVjj$ diagrams, whose forward tagging-jet kinematics are far more VBS-like, that boundary is not expected to transfer. Retraining the identical four-jet architecture on the correct target (version~2) isolates whether the limitation is one of training objective or of input information, while version~3 additionally includes the two large-$R$ signal jets, whose complementary discriminating power is established in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn} (Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_sigjet}), to test how much the decay-side information improves on the tagging jets alone.

These expectations are borne out on the combined diboson validation sample, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_roc} and summarised in Table~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_auc}. Version~1 reaches an AUC of only $0.629$ and lies close to the diagonal, confirming that its boundary does not transfer to the new objective. Retraining on the VBS versus non-VBS EWK target (version~2) raises the AUC substantially to $0.760$ from the very same four jets, demonstrating that the relevant topological information was accessible all along and that version~1's failure was one of objective, not of input. Adding the two large-$R$ signal jets (version~3) improves the AUC further to $0.801$, $+27.3\%$ relative to the version~1 baseline, with the additional gain over version~2 reflecting the complementary information carried by the diboson system established in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn}. The score distributions in Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_scores} show the same progression directly: version~1 leaves the two classes almost entirely overlapping, version~2 achieves a clear separation, and version~3 pushes non-VBS EWK events towards low scores and VBS events towards high scores, giving a markedly cleaner, bimodal separation.

Version~3 is therefore adopted as the baseline tagger for the remainder of the analysis. Beyond the inclusive AUC, this choice is reinforced by the response to the individual aQGC operator families discussed in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn_aqgc}: version~1 does not merely underperform inclusively but, as shown there, fails entirely to recognise the tensor- and mixed-operator topologies, whereas version~3 responds uniformly across all families, an operator independence that is essential given that a single tagger is applied to every signal hypothesis.

The inclusive gauges of performance translate directly into the operating point used downstream. Table~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_workingpoint} reports the VBS efficiency and non-VBS EWK rejection at the score threshold $s>0.3$ that defines the training selection (Table~\ref{tab:mlp_selection}), which moves the phase space close to the analysis signal region. At this threshold versions~2 and~3 both retain $\approx\!90\%$ of VBS events, whereas version~1 keeps only $57\%$: trained on a different objective, its VBS score is not pushed high enough for the loose threshold to be efficient, which alone disqualifies it as the analysis pre-selection. Comparing the two correctly trained taggers at their matched $\approx\!90\%$ VBS efficiency, version~3 rejects $20.8\%$ more non-VBS EWK background than version~2 in relative terms, equivalently, it accepts $12.5\%$ fewer background events, consistent with its higher AUC and its cleaner, more bimodal score distribution.

The combined AUC masks some channel dependence, which Table~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_auc_channels} and Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_roc_channels} resolve by diboson final state. The $WWjj$ and $WZjj$ channels follow the expected version ordering and account for most of the gain, version~3 improving on the version~1 baseline by $+30.6\%$ and $+19.5\%$ respectively. The $ZZjj$ channel behaves differently: version~1 already reaches $\mathrm{AUC}=0.790$, far above its $WW/WZ$ values, because the heavy scalar $H\to q\bar{q}q\bar{q}$ resonance on which version~1 was trained (Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn}) most closely resembles the $ZZjj$ final state, so retraining adds little and the six-jet version~3 is in fact marginally worse ($-0.9\%$). This is consistent with two effects: the combined six-jet model is optimised on the full sample, which is dominated by the higher-rate $WW$ and $WZ$ channels, and the additional signal-jet inputs carry little extra VBS-versus-non-VBS information for $ZZjj$ while slightly raising the model capacity. Because the analysis combines all three channels and is dominated by $WW$ and $WZ$, the combined version~3 tagger remains the appropriate choice, the $ZZjj$ behaviour is a known caveat rather than a problem for the inclusive selection.

\section{Response to the aQGC operators}
\label{sec:vbfrnn_aqgc}

The tagger is trained on inclusive electroweak $VVjj$ production rather than on any specific anomalous coupling, so it is essential to verify that it identifies the VBS topology consistently across the scalar, mixed and tensor aQGC operator families introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:sm}. These families excite different gauge-boson polarisation modes and modify the central diboson system differently, so they could in principle populate the tagger score differently even though they share the same VBS production topology.

Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_aqgc_v1} shows the response of version~1. The scalar operators are pushed to high scores, but the tensor and mixed operators peak at low scores. This is the operator-level manifestation of the train/target mismatch identified in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn_perf}: version~1 learned a boundary specific to the resonant VBF topology it was trained on, which resembles the scalar-operator kinematics but not the harder, transverse-polarisation-dominated configurations enhanced by the tensor and mixed operators. Version~1 has therefore learned a polarisation-specific proxy rather than the generic VBS topology, and its inclusive AUC deficit is seen here to be driven almost entirely by the tensor and mixed families.

Figures~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_aqgc_v2} and~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_aqgc_v3} show the same operators for version~2 and version~3. Version~2 already yields broad responses peaking at intermediate-to-high scores for all three families, with the input-multiplicity artefact of Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn_multiplicity} still visible. Version~3 shifts the response strongly towards high scores for every family: sharply peaked near $s\approx1$ for the scalar operators and broadly populating the high-score region for the tensor and mixed operators. The reason version~2 and version~3 are operator-independent where version~1 is not is that they were trained on the VBS production topology itself. The anomalous quartic vertex modifies the central diboson system, not the forward tagging jets that define VBS production, a tagger that keys on the production-side topology is therefore largely insensitive to the operator-dependent polarisation structure, which is exactly the behaviour observed. The residual difference, a sharp $s\approx1$ peak for the scalar operators versus a broader high-score distribution for the tensor and mixed operators, is consistent with the harder central kinematics of the transverse operators feeding into the signal-jet inputs, but it is immaterial for the analysis, since a loose score requirement retains all three families with high efficiency. This operator independence is what justifies using a single tagger, trained on inclusive EWK production, both as a pre-selection requirement and as an input feature to the aQGC discriminant of Chapter~\ref{chap:analysismethods}.

The visual impression is made quantitative in Tables~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_aqgc_meanscore} and~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_aqgc_eff}. For version~1 the mean score and the VBS-tag efficiency at $s>0.3$ are strongly operator-dependent: the scalar operators are tagged with $\approx\!91\%$ efficiency, unsurprisingly, since version~1 was itself trained on a scalar (Higgs) resonance whose decay bosons are longitudinally polarised, exactly the polarisation structure of the scalar operators, while the tensor and mixed operators are retained with only $36$--$43\%$ efficiency. Version~2 already removes most of this dependence and version~3 renders it negligible: every family is tagged with $94$--$98\%$ efficiency at a mean score of $0.74$--$0.87$. The relative gain from version~1 to version~3 is correspondingly modest for the scalar operators ($\approx\!8\%$) but exceeds $120\%$ for the tensor and mixed operators, so the improvement is concentrated exactly where version~1 failed. This quantitatively establishes the operator independence that justifies a single, inclusively trained tagger.

\section{Input jet multiplicity}
\label{sec:vbfrnn_multiplicity}

The version~2 score distribution in Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_scores} exhibits a localised artefact, a dip followed by a secondary peak near high score values, that is absent from the cleaner version~3 distribution. This structure is not of physical origin but reflects the number of jets actually available as input to the network. Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_njets} shows the version~2 score as a function of the input-jet multiplicity. A fraction of events reach the network with only a single tagging jet, because the remaining tagging jets fall outside the tracking and calorimeter acceptance or fail reconstruction. With a single jet the network has access to none of the inter-jet observables, the dijet mass, the rapidity gap, the relative angles, that carry the VBS information, it can place such events neither at the VBS nor at the non-VBS extreme and instead defaults to an uninformative band of intermediate scores. The pile-up of this single-jet population is precisely the structure seen in the version~2 distribution. Splitting the score by input multiplicity for each aQGC operator family, Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_njets_split}, confirms that the artefact originates entirely from the single-jet events: the $\geq2$-jet population is smoothly distributed and peaks at high score, while the single-jet curve is sharply confined to intermediate values for every family.

This also explains why version~3 is immune to the effect. The analysis pre-selection requires at least two large-$R$ signal jets (Table~\ref{tab:analysis_selection}), so these two jets are always available as input to the six-jet tagger, the single-jet configuration that produces the artefact therefore cannot occur for version~3 by construction, independently of the additional discriminating power that the signal jets provide. The removal of the artefact is thus a second, distinct reason, alongside the AUC gain, to prefer version~3.

The single-jet population responsible for the artefact is not a negligible tail. Across the aQGC operator families it accounts for $21$--$23\%$ of version~2 events (Table~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_njets_frac}), roughly one event in five, and its fraction is essentially independent of the operator. Guaranteeing a minimum input multiplicity in version~3 therefore recovers a substantial part of the sample from the uninformative intermediate-score band, which is reflected in the cleaner version~3 score distribution.

\section{Alternative transformer architecture}
\label{sec:vbfrnn_transformer}

Having fixed version~3 as the baseline, it is natural to ask whether the recurrent architecture itself limits the performance. The LSTM propagates information sequentially through the jet collection and can, in principle, lose distant context for long input sequences. A transformer encoder removes this sequential bottleneck by computing pairwise interactions between all input jets simultaneously through the self-attention mechanism described in Chapter~\ref{chap:ml}. To test whether this global structure offers a tangible benefit, a transformer encoder was trained on the identical six-jet input and VBS versus non-VBS EWK target as version~3. The transformer is a substantially higher-capacity model than the two-layer, width-$25$ LSTM of version~3, its architecture and hyperparameters are given in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn} (Table~\ref{tab:transformer_hyperparams}).

The result is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_roc_transformer} and Table~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_transformer_auc}. The transformer reaches an AUC of $0.806$, a marginal $+0.6\%$ relative to the version~3 RNN ($0.801$), the two ROC curves are essentially indistinguishable and, in particular, coincide in the high-VBS-efficiency regime that is operationally relevant for the analysis.

Several factors explain why the more expressive architecture does not translate into better discrimination here. First, the input sequence is very short, at most six jets. The only structural weakness of the recurrent network, the degradation of information flow across many time steps, does not arise over five intermediate steps: a two-layer LSTM already couples every jet to the event summary without meaningful loss, so self-attention's defining advantage, direct all-to-all coupling that bypasses sequential propagation, has almost nothing to act on. Second, the discriminating information is low-dimensional and largely additive. The VBS topology is encoded in a few robust global quantities, a forward tagging-jet pair with a large rapidity gap and large invariant mass, together with a central, hard diboson system, which a recurrent summary state already captures. When the optimal decision boundary is essentially a function of a handful of aggregate event variables, the higher-order relational modelling that attention provides cannot extract information that is not present in the inputs. Third, and as a consequence, the performance is limited by the intrinsic overlap between the VBS and non-VBS EWK classes rather than by model capacity: despite the substantially larger parameter count of the transformer (Table~\ref{tab:transformer_hyperparams}), both architectures plateau at $\mathrm{AUC}\approx0.80$ because they already sit close to the Bayes-optimal boundary achievable from these observables. The $\approx0.5\%$ difference is of the order of the statistical and training-procedure noise and is not reproduced in the high-efficiency regime, so it cannot be interpreted as a genuine architectural gain. Finally, transformers typically require larger training samples and more careful regularisation to realise their capacity advantage, with only four features per jet and the available statistics, the additional attention parameters add variance without a compensating reduction in bias.

Given the absence of a measurable benefit, and the additional model and inference complexity of the attention mechanism for the short jet sequences used here, the recurrent version~3 tagger is retained as the baseline for the analysis.

\chapter{Estimating the aQGC sensitivity using a machine-learning discriminant}
\label{chap:mldiscriminant_results}

This chapter evaluates the MLP-based aQGC discriminants introduced in Section~\ref{sec:mldiscriminant}. The binary and multi-class architectures are first compared at the score level on the training selection, after which the kinematic correlations learnt by the network and the relative importance of the input features are examined, and the chapter closes with a sensitivity study that turns these score distributions into expected significances. The MLP discriminants were developed in parallel with the VBF-RNN tagger, so four upstream configurations are reported throughout: VBF-RNN version~2 and version~3 (Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn}), each without and with the equal-signal-cross-section reweighting of Section~\ref{sec:mldiscriminant}. The last of these, VBF-RNN v3 combined with the cross-section reweighting, is the final configuration adopted for the analysis.

\section{Event selection}
\label{sec:event_selection}

All MLP results in this chapter are obtained on the training selection of Table~\ref{tab:mlp_selection}, which requires a VBF-RNN v3 score above $0.3$ on every event and a DisCoJet score above $0.65$ on both large-$R$ jets on top of the pre-selection of Table~\ref{tab:analysis_selection}. This phase space lies close to the analysis signal region while still retaining enough events to train and evaluate a network. The event yields obtained in this selection for the two upstream VBF-RNN versions are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:mlp_yields} for the three representative probe operators ($S_0$, $M_0$, $T_0$) and for each background component.

It is worth recalling what the upstream tagger does and does not contribute here. The VBF-RNN of Chapter~\ref{chap:vbfrnn_results} is a topology identifier: it separates the VBS subprocess from the non-VBS EWK $VVjj$ contributions to the same final state, and version~3 was shown there to identify the VBS topology more reliably than version~2 across every aQGC operator family. The separation of aQGC signal from the QCD-dominated analysis background is the job of the MLP discriminant introduced in this chapter, with the VBF-RNN score appearing as one of its input features.

Switching the upstream tagger from the four-jet version~2 to the six-jet version~3 leaves the individual probe-operator yields essentially unchanged (Table~\ref{tab:mlp_yields}, signal columns) but nearly doubles the total background yield ($+97.9\%$), driven by the QCD dijet component. The origin of this asymmetry is the input-multiplicity effect discussed in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn_multiplicity}: the six-jet network has access to the two large-$R$ signal jets that the four-jet network had to forgo, so it can score events that the four-jet network could only place in the uninformative intermediate-score band, and at the fixed working point $s_{\mathrm{VBF-RNN}}>0.3$ this acceptance gain falls predominantly on background. The cleaner v3 topology score (Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_scores}) is therefore paid for in upstream background yield, and the question of which upstream version best supports the downstream discriminant is decided not by the VBF-RNN AUC alone but by the Asimov sensitivity reported in Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity_comparison}.

A second methodological change applied during the iteration is the equal-signal-cross-section reweighting introduced in Section~\ref{sec:mldiscriminant}. The training already class-balances the categories the network sees: signal and background contribute equally to the binary cross-entropy, and each of $\{\,\mathrm{background},S,M,T\,\}$ contributes equally to the multi-class categorical cross-entropy. The remaining imbalance is at the level of the individual operators and decay channels \emph{within} each category. Per-operator signal cross sections span four orders of magnitude even within a single family, and within each operator the three diboson channels ($WW$, $WZ$, $ZZ$) contribute very differently, so the unweighted optimiser learns the high-rate operators and channels well while under-fitting the rare ones. The equal-signal-cross-section reweighting equalises the per-operator and per-channel cross sections at training time and restores the correct physical cross sections at evaluation, removing this residual imbalance. The footprint of this change differs between the binary and multi-class architectures, as discussed in Sections~\ref{sec:binary_global} and~\ref{sec:multiclass_classification}.

\section{Binary classification}
\label{sec:binary_classification}

The MLP is trained as a binary signal-versus-background classifier on the training selection of Section~\ref{sec:event_selection}. Each event is scored $s\in[0,1]$ approximating the posterior $P(\mathrm{signal}\,|\,\mathbf{x})$, and the final discriminant used for sensitivity estimation is the logit $\ln[p/(1-p)]$ that approaches the log-likelihood-ratio under the Neyman--Pearson lemma, as already introduced in Section~\ref{sec:mldiscriminant}. Two complementary training strategies are evaluated, specialised models trained on a single operator family at a time and a single global model trained jointly on the scalar, mixed and tensor families, and each is reported on the four upstream configurations introduced above. The inclusive AUCs are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:binary_auc}, while the score and ROC shapes for the v3 + equal-$\sigma$ configuration are shown in Figures~\ref{fig:binary_spec_scores}--\ref{fig:binary_global_score}.

\subsection{Specialised model}
\label{sec:binary_specialised}

Three independent classifiers are trained, on $\mathcal{O}_{S,0}+\mathcal{O}_{S,1}$, $\mathcal{O}_{M,0}+\mathcal{O}_{M,1}$ and $\mathcal{O}_{T,0}+\mathcal{O}_{T,1}$ respectively. On the final configuration they reach AUCs of $0.994$, $0.992$ and $0.992$ when probed with the lowest-index operator of each family. The score distributions in Figure~\ref{fig:binary_spec_scores} share a common pattern: signal peaks sharply at $s\approx1$, background at $s\approx0$, with a clean bimodal separation that is qualitatively very similar across the three operator families despite their different polarisation structure. The corresponding ROC curves of Figure~\ref{fig:binary_spec_roc} sit close to the top-left corner in each case.

Tracing the iteration columns of Table~\ref{tab:binary_auc} shows two effects. The change of upstream tagger from version~2 to version~3 lifts the specialised AUC by $+0.1$--$0.3\%$ across the three families, modest but uniform. The equal-signal-cross-section reweighting moves the specialised AUC by less than $\pm 0.1\%$: the specialised training mixture only spans two operators in a single family, which already share a similar effective cross section, so the rebalancing has nothing to correct and therefore no headline AUC footprint.

These AUC values look near-perfect, but the AUC alone is misleading here. The signal and the QCD-dominated background are very different physical processes, and the discriminating information is concentrated at the extreme low and high ends of the score distribution where the network has no difficulty separating the two classes. Figure~\ref{fig:binary_spec_counts}, which repeats the specialised score distributions on an absolute event scale for the three families, makes the imbalance explicit: the QCD-dominated background produces $\mathcal{O}(10^5)$ weighted events at low score, whereas the per-family signal yields are many orders of magnitude smaller, so even in the highest-score bin the absolute background exceeds the signal by a factor of $\mathcal{O}(10^2)$ for the scalar and mixed probe operators and only approaches comparable yields for the high-cross-section tensor probe operator. AUC, which weights all thresholds equally, therefore over-states what can be inferred about the analysis sensitivity. The genuine question, whether the cleaner v3 score shape is worth the doubled background yield it accepts, is decided by the Asimov significance in Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity_comparison}.

\subsection{Global model}
\label{sec:binary_global}

A single MLP is trained jointly on all available aQGC operators across the three families, $S(0\text{--}1) + M(0\text{--}5,\,7) + T(0\text{--}9)$. On the final configuration it reaches AUCs of $0.993$, $0.992$ and $0.991$ when probed with $S_0$, $M_0$ and $T_0$ respectively, so per family the global model loses at most $\approx 0.1\%$ AUC relative to its specialised counterpart. Carrying a single decision boundary across the entire operator space is therefore effectively free in AUC, while greatly reducing operational complexity: one trained network covers every operator hypothesis, including those that the specialised models never saw at training time.

Tracing the iteration columns of Table~\ref{tab:binary_auc} now shows a clear footprint for the equal-signal-cross-section reweighting. The global $S_0$ AUC rises by $+0.3\%$ at both upstream versions, from $0.986$ to $0.989$ at v2 and from $0.990$ to $0.993$ at v3, while the $M_0$ and $T_0$ AUCs are unchanged within rounding. The binary classifier collapses every aQGC operator into a single signal category, so within that category the high-cross-section tensor (and to a lesser extent mixed) operators dominate the unweighted optimisation and the scalar operators contribute relatively little. The equal-signal-cross-section reweighting restores their contribution at training time, recovering the scalar classification at no cost to the mixed or tensor families.

The score distribution for the final configuration, Figure~\ref{fig:binary_global_score}, makes the operator-inclusive behaviour explicit: $S_0$, $M_0$ and $T_0$ peak together near $s\approx1$ and lie close enough to one another that a single score generalises well across the three families. The absolute-scale counterpart, Figure~\ref{fig:binary_global_counts}, repeats the AUC caveat already encountered for the specialised models: even on the inclusive signal mixture the background-to-signal ratio in every bin is at least $\mathcal{O}(10^2)$ and reaches $\mathcal{O}(10^4)$ at low score, so the inclusive AUC is not a useful proxy for the expected sensitivity. As for the specialised models, the genuine performance comparison is deferred to Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity_comparison}.

\section{Multi-class classification}
\label{sec:multiclass_classification}

The binary classifiers of Section~\ref{sec:binary_classification} reduce every event to a single signal-versus-background score and discard any information about which operator family is responsible. The multi-class architecture introduced in Section~\ref{sec:mldiscriminant} keeps that information by replacing the single sigmoid output with a four-node softmax over $\{\,\mathrm{background},\,S,\,M,\,T\,\}$, trained on every available aQGC operator simultaneously. The behaviour of the four output nodes on the v3 + equal-$\sigma$ configuration is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:multiclass_scores}. The background node behaves as a clean signal-versus-background discriminant, mirroring the global binary model with $S_0$, $M_0$ and $T_0$ piling up at low $\hat{y}_{\mathrm{bkg}}$ and the composite background at high $\hat{y}_{\mathrm{bkg}}$. The scalar node separates the scalar family well: $S_0$ peaks at high $\hat{y}_S$, the transverse $M_0$ and $T_0$ sit at intermediate values, and the background falls off towards low $\hat{y}_S$. The mixed and tensor nodes, however, are essentially degenerate: $M_0$ and $T_0$ peak together at the same $\hat{y}_M$ and $\hat{y}_T$ values, with $S_0$ and background sitting below in both, so neither node can reliably tell the two transverse-polarisation operator families apart from the input features used here. This is consistent with the shared transverse-mode origin of the mixed and tensor operators discussed in Chapter~\ref{chap:sm}.

The equal-signal-cross-section reweighting is most consequential for this classifier, but for a different reason than for the global binary model of Section~\ref{sec:binary_global}. The four softmax categories $\{\,\mathrm{background},S,M,T\,\}$ are already class-balanced at training time, so the imbalance the reweighting addresses sits one level deeper, inside each of the three signal categories. The scalar category lumps $S_0$ and $S_1$ together at very different cross sections, the mixed category covers $M(0\text{--}5,\,7)$ over roughly two orders of magnitude in cross section, and the tensor category covers $T(0\text{--}9)$ over a similar dynamic range; within every operator the three diboson decay channels ($WW$, $WZ$, $ZZ$) contribute very differently as well. Without rebalancing, the network learns each category from its highest-cross-section operators and channels and under-fits the rare ones. Operator-level sensitivity scans on the unbalanced training confirm the symptom: the per-operator sensitivities for the low-cross-section operators (notably $S_0$ and $S_1$) flatten out near the background-only baseline at both v2 and v3, and recover to comparable levels across the operator space once the per-operator and per-channel cross sections are balanced at training time. The improvement is uniform across the two upstream tagger versions, so the reweighting is best understood as an orthogonal training-time fix rather than a v3-specific correction. The physical cross sections are restored at evaluation, and v3 + equal-$\sigma$ is the final configuration adopted for the analysis.

Even with the $M/T$ degeneracy, this architecture is potentially powerful for the analysis. The four output nodes provide four complementary discriminating directions from a single trained model, so the multi-class classifier can supply a different best-separating discriminant for each probed operator without retraining: the scalar node strongly separates $S$, the mixed and tensor nodes both separate the transverse-polarisation operators from background, and the background node provides a generic signal-versus-background score. Whether this multiplicity of discriminants translates into a sensitivity gain over the global binary classifier is decided again by the Asimov significance reported in Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity_comparison}.

The background is recovered cleanly ($94\%$) and the scalar family reasonably well ($78\%$), but the mixed and tensor classes are heavily cross-assigned: only $22\%$ of true mixed events are labelled mixed, with $32\%$ migrating to the scalar and $42\%$ to the tensor node, and true tensor events are recovered with only $56\%$ efficiency. This is the confusion-matrix manifestation of the mixed/tensor degeneracy already seen in the output node distributions of Figure~\ref{fig:multiclass_scores}.

The confusion matrix is, however, not the best gauge of performance for this analysis, for the same reason discussed above for the output node distributions. It forces a hard four-way argmax assignment over $\{\,\mathrm{background},S,M,T\,\}$, whereas the multi-class discriminant is never used that way: the analysis does not employ multiple signal output nodes simultaneously, but probes one operator at a time, retaining only the output node matching the family of the probed operator and using it, together with the background, as a binary signal-versus-background discriminant. The analysis therefore never needs to tell the scalar, mixed and tensor families apart from one another, so the large mixed/tensor cross-assignments that dominate the off-diagonal do not propagate into the sensitivity. As for the binary classifiers, the genuine performance comparison is the Asimov significance of Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity_comparison}.

\section{Feature ranking}
\label{sec:feature_ranking}

The MLP discriminants of the previous sections each take the twelve event-level input features defined in Section~\ref{sec:mldiscriminant}: the transverse momentum, pseudorapidity, azimuthal angle and invariant mass of each of the two leading large-$R$ signal jets, the VBF-RNN version~3 score, and three multiplicity variables, the number of RNN tagging jets, the total number of small-$R$ jets and the number of large-$R$ jets. These inputs do not contribute equally to the learnt decision boundary. Quantifying their relative contribution serves two purposes: it provides a physical cross-check that the network keys on the kinematic handles expected to carry the aQGC information, and it identifies redundant inputs that carry little or no weight and could in principle be dropped.

\subsection{Permutation importance}
\label{sec:fr_method}

The relative importance of the input features is assessed with \emph{permutation importance}, a model-agnostic procedure that probes how strongly a trained network relies on each feature without retraining it. Let $f$ denote the trained discriminant and let $A_0$ be a performance metric evaluated on the validation sample $\mathcal{D}=\{(\mathbf{x}_i,y_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$; throughout this section the metric is the AUC introduced in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn_eval}. For a given input feature $j$, a perturbed sample $\mathcal{D}^{\pi_j}$ is built by randomly permuting the values of that feature across the $N$ validation events,
\begin{equation}
  x^{(j)}_i \;\longrightarrow\; x^{(j)}_{\pi(i)},
  \qquad \pi\ \text{a random permutation of}\ \{1,\dots,N\},
\end{equation}
while every other feature and the labels are left untouched. The permutation destroys any association between feature $j$ and the label but leaves its marginal distribution intact, so the network is evaluated on inputs that remain individually realistic but in which feature $j$ has been rendered uninformative. Re-evaluating the frozen network on $\mathcal{D}^{\pi_j}$ yields a degraded metric $A_j$, and the importance of the feature is the resulting drop
\begin{equation}
  \Delta A_j \;=\; A_0 - A_j \;\ge\; 0,
\end{equation}
which is large for a feature the decision boundary depends on and consistent with zero for one the network ignores. To compare features on a common scale the drops are normalised to unit sum,
\begin{equation}
  I_j \;=\; \frac{\Delta A_j}{\sum_{k}\Delta A_k},
  \label{eq:rel_importance}
\end{equation}
and $I_j$ is quoted below as the relative feature importance. The same information is shown graphically by the ROC curves of the perturbed networks: each ``without feature $X$'' curve is the ROC obtained on the corresponding $\mathcal{D}^{\pi_j}$, and the area under it is $A_j$.

Two properties of the method matter for the interpretation of the rankings. First, because the network is never retrained, $I_j$ measures the reliance of \emph{this particular trained model} on feature $j$ rather than the intrinsic separating power of the variable: a feature that is informative but whose information is also carried by a correlated partner can be permuted with little metric loss, and is then assigned a low importance. Permutation importance therefore tends to share, and partly mask, the importance of correlated inputs, an effect examined further in the correlation study of Section~\ref{sec:correlation_studies}. Second, the procedure is stochastic through the choice of permutation $\pi$; the values quoted here are averaged over repeated permutations so that the residual scatter is small compared with the differences between features.

\subsection{Specialised models}
\label{sec:fr_specialised}

The permutation importance of the three specialised binary classifiers is shown in Table~\ref{tab:fr_rank_spec}. All three models behave the same way at the coarsest level: the large-$R$ signal-jet kinematics dominate, while the two azimuthal angles $\phi$ carry no importance whatsoever and the pseudorapidities and the small-$R$/large-$R$ jet multiplicities contribute at most a per cent each. The vanishing $\phi$ importance is expected from the azimuthal symmetry of the detector, which makes the $\phi$ distributions flat for both signal and background (Figure~\ref{fig:input_distributions}); these inputs could be dropped from the discriminant with no loss of performance.

The informative variable dependence lies in how the importance is distributed among the signal-jet kinematics, and it tracks the polarisation structure of the operator families (Chapter~\ref{chap:sm}). For the scalar classifier the two signal-jet masses dominate, jointly carrying $54\%$ of the importance, with the leading-jet $p_T$ third at $18\%$. For the mixed and tensor classifiers the ordering is reversed: the leading signal-jet $p_T$ alone carries $45\%$ and $57\%$ respectively, and the jet masses are demoted. The transverse-polarisation mixed and tensor operators enhance the high-energy tail of the diboson system strongly, so the leading-jet $p_T$ becomes the single most powerful handle, whereas the longitudinal scalar operators produce a milder $p_T$ enhancement and the discrimination leans instead on the boson-jet mass, which peaks in the $W/Z$ mass window for signal. The VBF-RNN score is a modest contributor ($11\%$ for the scalar model, $2$--$3\%$ for the transverse families): it is itself a strong tagger, but the training selection already requires a score above $0.3$, so the surviving events are RNN-enriched and the residual discrimination the score can still provide is correspondingly reduced.

Table~\ref{tab:fr_specialised_auc} shows that the classifiers are robust to the loss of any single feature: the baseline AUC of $0.991$--$0.994$ falls by at most $\approx0.02$ for the most important input and is unchanged to three decimals when a $\phi$ variable is removed. The largest single drop is for the leading-jet $p_T$ in the tensor model ($0.991\to0.933$) and for the leading-jet mass in the scalar model ($0.994\to0.972$), consistent with the importance ordering.

\subsection{Global model}
\label{sec:fr_global}

The global binary classifier is trained jointly on all aQGC operators across the three families. Its permutation importance, Figure~\ref{fig:fr_rank_global}, is intermediate between the scalar and the transverse specialised models: the leading signal-jet $p_T$ is the single most important feature at $41\%$, but the two jet masses together still account for $39\%$. This reflects the composition of the joint training set, $S(0\text{--}1)+M(0\text{--}5,\,7)+T(0\text{--}9)$, which is numerically dominated by transverse operators, so the $p_T$-driven transverse behaviour prevails, while the equal-signal-cross-section reweighting restores the scalar contribution and keeps the jet masses relevant, in line with the discussion of Section~\ref{sec:binary_global}. The remaining inputs follow the specialised pattern: the number of RNN jets contributes $8\%$, the VBF-RNN score and the sub-leading $p_T$ about $4\%$ each, and the $\eta$, $\phi$ and small-/large-$R$ multiplicity variables are negligible. As for the specialised models the classifier is robust to the loss of any single input: the baseline AUC of $0.991$ degrades by at most $0.036$, when the leading signal-jet $p_T$ is permuted, and is unchanged when a $\phi$ variable is removed (Table~\ref{tab:fr_global_auc} and Figure~\ref{fig:fr_roc_global}).

\subsection{Multi-class model}
\label{sec:fr_multiclass}

For the multi-class classifier the permutation importance is evaluated separately for each pair of classes: the validation sample is restricted to the two classes in question and the metric $A_0$ in Equation~\ref{eq:rel_importance} is the corresponding one-versus-one AUC. The six pairs split naturally into the three signal-versus-background tasks and the three inter-signal tasks, and the two groups behave very differently.

The signal-versus-background rankings, Table~\ref{tab:fr_rank_mc_bkg}, reproduce the specialised and global results almost exactly: the scalar-versus-background axis is driven by the jet masses, the mixed- and tensor-versus-background axes by the leading signal-jet $p_T$, and the one-versus-one AUCs ($0.994$, $0.991$, $0.991$) match those of the dedicated binary classifiers. This is a strong consistency check that the four-node softmax learns the same signal-versus-background physics as the binary models while additionally providing the operator-family information.

The inter-signal tasks, Table~\ref{tab:fr_rank_mc_sig}, are a different matter. Their baseline AUCs are much lower (Figure~\ref{fig:fr_roc_mc_sig}), $0.841$ for tensor versus scalar, $0.804$ for mixed versus scalar and only $0.602$ for tensor versus mixed, the last being the feature-ranking manifestation of the mixed/tensor degeneracy already seen in the output node distributions (Figure~\ref{fig:multiclass_scores}) and the confusion matrix (Figure~\ref{fig:multiclass_cm}). The variable dependence also changes: the jet masses, which dominate the signal-versus-background separation, lose almost all importance, because the two transverse families share essentially the same boson content and the mass therefore cannot tell them apart. Discrimination shifts onto the leading signal-jet $p_T$ together with the sub-leading-jet $p_T$ and the number of large-$R$ jets. For the scalar-versus-transverse tasks a dominant handle still exists, the leading-jet $p_T$ carrying $40$--$47\%$, but for tensor versus mixed the importance profile is essentially flat: the leading-jet $p_T$ falls to $29\%$ and the jet multiplicities ($16\%$ and $14\%$ for the number of RNN and large-$R$ jets) and even the pseudorapidities ($\approx10\%$ each) become comparably important. A flat ranking with no dominant feature is exactly what is expected when two classes are nearly inseparable, and Table~\ref{tab:fr_mc_auc} confirms it quantitatively: removing the leading-jet $p_T$ lowers the tensor-versus-mixed AUC only from $0.602$ to $0.570$, so the modest residual separation is spread thinly across many weak handles rather than concentrated in any one of them. As in every other model the $\phi$ variables remain uninformative throughout.
