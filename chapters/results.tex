\chapter{Performance of a VBS topology identifier}
\label{chap:vbfrnn_results}

This chapter evaluates the VBF-RNN tagger introduced in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn} on its ability to isolate the vector boson scattering topology from the inclusive electroweak $VVjj$ production. The three RNN versions of Table~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_versions} are first compared on a common gauge of performance, after which the artefacts introduced by the jet multiplicity are discussed and the response of the tagger to the different aQGC operator families is studied, finally an alternative transformer-based architecture is evaluated. The performance reported here motivates the choice of the version~3 score as both a selection requirement and an input feature to the aQGC discriminant of Chapter~\ref{chap:analysismethods}.

\section{Event selection and evaluation}
\label{sec:vbfrnn_eval}

The tagger is evaluated on the statistically independent validation sample of electroweak $VVjj$ events described in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn}, using the truth-level VBS / non-VBS labels. The $WW$, $WZ$ and $ZZ$ final states are combined to reflect the overall VBF-RNN performance, the channel by channel breakdown is given in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn_perf}.

Because the tagger output is a continuous score $s\in[0,1]$, its discriminating power is quantified with the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve. For a given threshold $s_{\mathrm{cut}}$, events with $s>s_{\mathrm{cut}}$ are tagged as VBS. The true-positive rate (the VBS efficiency, or VBS positive rate) is the fraction of true VBS events that are tagged, while the false-positive rate (the non-VBS positive rate) is the fraction of non-VBS EWK events that are also tagged. Sweeping $s_{\mathrm{cut}}$ from $1$ to $0$ traces out the ROC curve in the (false-positive rate, true-positive rate) plane. A perfect classifier reaches the top-left corner, whereas a random classifier lies on the diagonal. The area under this curve (AUC) condenses the curve into a single scalar: it equals the probability that a randomly chosen VBS event is assigned a higher score than a randomly chosen non-VBS event, so that $\mathrm{AUC}=1$ is perfect separation and $\mathrm{AUC}=0.5$ is no separation. The AUC is used throughout this chapter as the primary gauge of performance.

\section{Comparison of the tagger versions}
\label{sec:vbfrnn_perf}

The three VBF-RNN versions of Table~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_versions} differ both in their training target and in the set of input jets and the comparison below is constructed to disentangle the impact of each. Version~1 was trained on a different objective, resonant VBF against gluon-fusion production, so the decision boundary it learned reflects that problem rather than the VBS versus non-VBS EWK separation required here. When the negative class changes from gluon-fusion jets to the other electroweak $VVjj$ diagrams, whose forward tagging-jet kinematics are far more VBS-like, that boundary is not expected to transfer. Retraining the identical four-jet architecture on the correct target (version~2) isolates whether the limitation is one of training objective or of input information, while version~3 additionally includes the two large-$R$ signal jets, whose complementary discriminating power is established in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn} (Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_sigjet}), to test how much the decay-side information improves on the tagging jets alone.

These expectations are confirmed on the combined diboson validation sample, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_roc} and summarised in Table~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_auc}. Version~1 reaches an AUC of only $0.629$ and lies close to the diagonal, confirming that its boundary does not transfer to the new objective. Retraining on the VBS versus non-VBS EWK target (version~2) raises the AUC substantially to $0.760$ from the very same four jets, demonstrating that the relevant topological information was accessible all along and that version~1's failure was one of objective, not of input. Adding the two large-$R$ signal jets (version~3) improves the AUC further to $0.801$, $+27.3\%$ relative to the version~1 baseline, with the additional gain over version~2 reflecting the complementary information carried by the diboson system established in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn}. The score distributions in Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_scores} show the same progression directly: version~1 leaves the two classes almost entirely overlapping, version~2 achieves a clear separation and version~3 pushes non-VBS EWK events towards low scores and VBS events towards high scores.

Version~3 is therefore adopted as the baseline tagger for the remainder of the analysis. Beyond the inclusive AUC, this choice is reinforced by the response to the individual aQGC operator families discussed in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn_aqgc}: version~1 does not merely underperform inclusively but, as shown there, fails entirely to recognise the tensor- and mixed-operator topologies, whereas version~3 responds uniformly across all families, an operator independence that is essential given that a single tagger is applied to every signal hypothesis.

The inclusive gauges of performance translate directly into the operating point used downstream. Table~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_workingpoint} reports the VBS efficiency and non-VBS EWK rejection at the score threshold $s>0.3$ that defines the training selection (Table~\ref{tab:mlp_selection}). At this threshold versions~2 and~3 both retain $\approx\!90\%$ of VBS events, whereas version~1 keeps only $57\%$. Trained on a different objective, version~1 does not push its VBS score high enough for the loose threshold to be efficient, which alone disqualifies it as the analysis pre-selection. Comparing the two correctly trained taggers at their matched $\approx\!90\%$ VBS efficiency, version~3 rejects $20.8\%$ more non-VBS EWK background than version~2 in relative terms. Equivalently, version~3 accepts $12.5\%$ fewer background events, consistent with its higher AUC and its cleaner, more bimodal score distribution.

The combined AUC masks some channel dependence, which Table~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_auc_channels} and Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_roc_channels} resolve by diboson final state. The $WWjj$ and $WZjj$ channels follow the expected version ordering and account for most of the gain, version~3 improving on the version~1 baseline by $+30.6\%$ and $+19.5\%$ respectively. The $ZZjj$ channel behaves differently: version~1 already reaches $\mathrm{AUC}=0.790$, far above its $WW/WZ$ values, so retraining adds little and the six-jet version~3 is in fact marginally worse ($-0.9\%$). This is consistent with two effects: the combined six-jet model is optimised on the full sample, which is dominated by the higher-rate $WW$ and $WZ$ channels and the additional signal-jet inputs carry little extra VBS versus non-VBS information for $ZZjj$ while slightly raising the model capacity. Because the analysis combines all three channels and is dominated by $WW$ and $WZ$, the combined version~3 tagger remains the appropriate choice.

\section{Response to the aQGC operators}
\label{sec:vbfrnn_aqgc}

The tagger is trained on inclusive electroweak $VVjj$ production rather than on any specific anomalous coupling, so it is essential to verify that it identifies the VBS topology consistently across the scalar, mixed and tensor aQGC operator families introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:sm}. These families excite different gauge-boson polarisation modes and modify the central diboson system differently, so they could in principle populate the tagger score differently even though they share the same VBS production topology.

Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_aqgc_v1} shows the response of version~1. The scalar operators are pushed to high scores, but the tensor and mixed operators peak at low scores. This is the operator-level manifestation of the train/target mismatch identified in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn_perf}: version~1 learned a boundary specific to the resonant VBF topology it was trained on, which resembles the scalar-operator kinematics but not the harder, transverse polarisation dominated configurations enhanced by the tensor and mixed operators.

Figures~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_aqgc_v2} and~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_aqgc_v3} show the same operators for version~2 and version~3. Version~2 correctly identifies all operator classes as signal. Version~3 shifts the response strongly towards high scores for every family, sharply peaked near $s\approx1$ for the scalar operators and broadly populating the high-score region for the tensor and mixed operators. The reason version~2 and version~3 are operator-independent where version~1 is not is that they were trained on the VBS production topology itself. The anomalous quartic vertex modifies the central diboson system, not the forward tagging jets that define VBS production, a tagger that depends on the production-side topology is therefore largely insensitive to the operator-dependent polarisation structure, which is exactly the behaviour observed. The residual difference, a sharp $s\approx1$ peak for the scalar operators versus a broader high-score distribution for the tensor and mixed operators, is immaterial for the analysis, since a loose score requirement retains all three families with high efficiency. This operator independence is what justifies using a single tagger, trained on inclusive EWK production, both as a selection requirement and as an input feature to the aQGC discriminant of Chapter~\ref{chap:analysismethods}.

The visual impression is made quantitative in Tables~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_aqgc_meanscore} and~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_aqgc_eff}. For version~1 the mean score and the VBS-tag efficiency at $s>0.3$ are strongly operator-dependent. The scalar operators are tagged with $\approx\!91\%$ efficiency, as expected. The tensor and mixed operators are retained with only 36\% to 43\% efficiency. Version~2 already removes most of this dependence and version~3 renders it negligible, every family is tagged with 94\% to 98\% efficiency at a mean score between 0.74 and 0.87. The relative gain from version~1 to version~3 is correspondingly modest for the scalar operators ($\approx\!8\%$) but exceeds $120\%$ for the tensor and mixed operators, so the improvement is concentrated where version~1 failed.

\section{Input jet multiplicity}
\label{sec:vbfrnn_multiplicity}

The version~2 score distribution in Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_scores} shows a localised artefact, a secondary peak near $s\approx0.6$, that is absent from the version~3 distribution. This structure is not of physical origin but reflects the number of jets actually available as input to the network. Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_njets} shows the version~2 score as a function of the input-jet multiplicity. A fraction of events reach the network with only a single tagging jet, because the remaining tagging jets fall outside the tracking and calorimeter acceptance or fail reconstruction. With a single jet the network has access to none of the inter-jet observables, the dijet mass, the rapidity gap, the relative angles, that carry the VBS information, it can place such events neither at the VBS nor at the non-VBS extreme and instead defaults to an uninformative band of intermediate scores. The pile-up of this single-jet population is precisely the structure seen in the version~2 distribution. Splitting the score by input multiplicity for each aQGC operator family, Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_njets_split}, confirms that the artefact originates entirely from the single-jet events: the $\geq2$-jet population is smoothly distributed and peaks at high score, while the single-jet curve is sharply confined to intermediate values for every family.

This also explains why version~3 is immune to the effect. The analysis pre-selection requires at least two large-$R$ signal jets (Table~\ref{tab:analysis_selection}), so these two jets are always available in addition to the required single tagging jet as input to the six-jet tagger, the single-jet configuration that produces the artefact therefore cannot occur for version~3 by construction, independently of the additional discriminating power that the signal jets provide. The removal of the artefact is thus a second, distinct reason, alongside the AUC gain, to prefer version~3.

The single-jet population responsible for the artefact is not a negligible tail. Across the aQGC operator families it accounts for 21\% to 23\% of events (Table~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_njets_frac}), roughly one event in five and its fraction is essentially independent of the operator. Guaranteeing a minimum input multiplicity of three jets in version~3 therefore recovers a substantial part of the sample from the intermediate-score band.

\section{Alternative transformer architecture}
\label{sec:vbfrnn_transformer}

Having fixed version~3 as the baseline, it is natural to ask whether the recurrent architecture itself limits the performance. The LSTM propagates information sequentially through the jet collection and can, in principle, lose distant context for long input sequences. A transformer architecture removes this sequential bottleneck by computing pairwise interactions between all input jets simultaneously through the self-attention mechanism described in Chapter~\ref{chap:ml}. To test whether this global structure offers a tangible benefit, a transformer model was trained on the identical six-jet input and VBS versus non-VBS EWK target as version~3. The transformer is a substantially higher-capacity model than the two-layer, width-$25$ LSTM of version~3, its architecture and hyperparameters are given in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn} (Table~\ref{tab:transformer_hyperparams}).

The result is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:vbfrnn_roc_transformer} and Table~\ref{tab:vbfrnn_transformer_auc}. The transformer reaches an AUC of $0.806$, a marginal $+0.6\%$ relative to the version~3 RNN ($0.801$), the two ROC curves are essentially indistinguishable.

Several factors explain why the more expressive architecture does not translate into better discrimination here. First, the input sequence is short, at most six jets. The only structural weakness of the recurrent network, the degradation of information flow across many time steps, does not arise over five intermediate steps. A two-layer LSTM already couples every jet to the event summary without meaningful loss, so self-attention's defining advantage of direct all to all coupling has almost nothing to act on. Second, the discriminating information is low-dimensional and largely additive. The VBS topology is encoded in a few robust global quantities: a forward tagging-jet pair with a large rapidity gap and large invariant mass, together with a central, hard diboson system, which a recurrent summary state already captures. When the optimal decision boundary is essentially a function of a handful of aggregate event variables, the higher-order relational modelling that attention provides cannot extract information that is not present in the inputs. Third, the performance is limited by the intrinsic overlap between the VBS and non-VBS EWK classes rather than by model capacity. The $0.6\%$ difference is of the order of the statistical and training-procedure noise and cannot be interpreted as a genuine architectural gain. Finally, transformers typically require larger training samples and more careful regularisation to realise their capacity advantage. With only four features per jet and the available statistics, the additional attention parameters add variance without a compensating reduction in bias.

Given the absence of a measurable benefit and the additional inference complexity, the transformer model is rejected for use in the analysis.

\chapter{Estimating the aQGC sensitivity using a machine-learning discriminant}
\label{chap:mldiscriminant_results}

This chapter evaluates the MLP-based aQGC discriminants introduced in Section~\ref{sec:mldiscriminant}. The binary and multi-class architectures are first compared at the score level on the training selection, after which the kinematic correlations learnt by the network and the relative importance of the input features are examined and the chapter closes with a sensitivity study that turns these score distributions into expected significances. The MLP discriminants were developed in parallel with the VBF-RNN tagger, so four upstream configurations are reported throughout: VBF-RNN version~2 and version~3 (Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn}), each without and with the equal signal cross-section reweighting of Section~\ref{sec:mldiscriminant}. 

\section{Event selection}
\label{sec:event_selection}

All MLP results in this chapter are obtained on the training selection of Table~\ref{tab:mlp_selection}, which requires a VBF-RNN score above $0.3$ on every event and a DisCoJet score above $0.65$ on both large-$R$ jets on top of the pre-selection of Table~\ref{tab:analysis_selection}. This phase space lies close to the analysis signal region while still retaining enough events to train and evaluate a network. The event yields obtained in this selection for the two upstream VBF-RNN versions are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:mlp_yields} for the three representative probe operators ($S_0$, $M_0$, $T_0$) and for each background component.

It is worth recalling what the upstream tagger does and does not contribute here. The VBF-RNN of Chapter~\ref{chap:vbfrnn_results} is a topology identifier: it separates the VBS subprocess from the non-VBS EWK $VVjj$ contributions to the same final state and version~3 was shown there to identify the VBS topology more reliably than version~2. The separation of aQGC signal from the QCD-dominated analysis background is the job of the MLP discriminant introduced in this chapter, with the VBF-RNN score appearing as one of its input features.

At the fixed working point $s_{\mathrm{VBF-RNN}}>0.3$ switching the upstream tagger from the four-jet version~2 to the six-jet version~3 leaves the individual probe-operator yields essentially unchanged (Table~\ref{tab:mlp_yields}, signal columns), but nearly doubles the total background yield ($+97.9\%$), driven by the QCD dijet component.

A second methodological change applied during the iteration is the equal signal cross-section reweighting introduced in Section~\ref{sec:mldiscriminant}. The training already class-balances the categories the network sees: signal and background contribute equally to the binary cross-entropy and each of $\{\,\mathrm{background},S,M,T\,\}$ contributes equally to the multi-class categorical cross-entropy. The remaining imbalance is at the level of the individual operators and decay channels within each category. Per-operator signal cross sections span four orders of magnitude even within a single family and within each operator the three diboson channels ($WW$, $WZ$, $ZZ$) contribute very differently, so the unweighted optimiser learns the high-rate operators and channels well while under-fitting the rare ones. The equal signal cross-section reweighting equalises the per-operator and per-channel cross sections at training time and restores the correct physical cross sections at evaluation. The results of this change differs between the binary and multi-class architectures, as discussed in Sections~\ref{sec:binary_global} and~\ref{sec:multiclass_classification}.

The chosen training-selection cuts, a VBF-RNN v3 score above $0.3$ on every event and a DisCoJet score above $0.65$ on both large-$R$ jets, are not the only viable working points. Tighter cuts purify the sample at the cost of signal yield and the trade-off must be set by the metric that actually drives the analysis, the Asimov significance $Z_A$ of the downstream MLP discriminant given in Section \ref{final_discirminant}. To establish the working-point choice on physics grounds rather than convention, the global binary MLP of Section~\ref{sec:binary_global} on the final v3~+~equal-$\sigma$ configuration is retrained from scratch under each alternative selection, scanning the VBF-RNN cut over $\{0.3,0.5,0.7,0.9\}$ at fixed DisCoJet${}>0.65$ and the DisCoJet cut over $\{0.65,0.85\}$ at fixed VBF-RNN${}>0.3$. Crucially this is a training-selection scan, not a re-evaluation of a trained network on different test selections, so for every point in the scan a new MLP is trained on the events surviving that selection and the resulting Asimov significance reflects the best discriminant the network can extract from the available training statistics. The resulting $Z_A$ values for the three representative probe operators $S_0$, $M_0$ and $T_0$ are shown in Figures~\ref{fig:wp_vbfrnn} and~\ref{fig:wp_bt} and the corresponding relative differences against the chosen working point are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:wp_scan}.

The VBF-RNN scan in Figure~\ref{fig:wp_vbfrnn} reveals two opposing trends. For the rare scalar operator $S_0$, tighter VBF-RNN cuts steadily improve $Z_A$, from $0.870$ at the chosen ${>}0.3$ working point to $1.095$ at ${>}0.9$, a $+25.9\%$ gain that reflects the larger fractional background reduction in a regime where the signal yield is already small but the per-event $S/B$ remains the limiting factor. The mixed and tensor operators show the opposite behaviour: $Z_A$ falls monotonically with the cut, by $34.7\%$ on $M_0$ and $37.1\%$ on $T_0$ at the tightest setting. The analysis should reach all three families with a single selection and the chosen ${>}0.3$ cut is the one working point on the scan that keeps the high cross-section $M_0$ and $T_0$ near their maximum while losing only $\sim$$26\%$ on the rare $S_0$ which sits at $Z_A\sim 1$ regardless and is therefore irrelevant to the headline sensitivity of the analysis.

For the DisCoJet scan in Figure~\ref{fig:wp_bt} , the chosen ${>}0.65$ cut is equivalent to the tight cut on the scalar operator (within $\pm 1\%$), modestly better on the mixed operator $M_0$ with a $-8.2\%$ loss at the tighter cut and strictly better on the dominant tensor operator $T_0$ with $Z_A=89.85$ at ${>}0.65$ versus $72.15$ at ${>}0.85$, a $-19.7\%$ loss at the tighter cut.

The chosen pair therefore sits at the natural plateau on every operator family and tightening either cut sacrifices the dominant tensor and mixed sensitivities in exchange for at most a marginal gain on a scalar operator that is well below discovery in any case. The working-point pair (VBF-RNN${}>0.3$, DisCoJet${}>0.65$) is consequently the choice adopted for the rest of this thesis.

With the training selection now fixed, the next four sections characterise the trained networks themselves through the standard machine-learning metrics: AUC values, ROC curves, confusion matrices, permutation feature importance and input correlations. The Asimov significance used in this subsection as a working-point calibration is set aside until then and the full operator by operator sensitivity comparison across all four upstream configurations returns in Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity_comparison}.

\section{Binary classification}
\label{sec:binary_classification}

The MLP is trained as a binary signal versus background classifier on the training selection of Section~\ref{sec:event_selection}. Each event is scored $s\in[0,1]$ approximating the posterior $P(\mathrm{signal}\,|\,\mathbf{x})$. The final discriminant used for sensitivity estimation is the logit $\ln[p/(1-p)]$, as already introduced in Section~\ref{sec:mldiscriminant}. Two complementary training strategies are evaluated: specialised models trained on a single operator family at a time and a single global model trained jointly on the scalar, mixed and tensor families. Each strategy is reported on the four upstream configurations introduced above. The inclusive AUCs are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:binary_auc}, while the score and ROC shapes for the v3 + equal-$\sigma$ configuration are shown in Figures~\ref{fig:binary_spec_scores} to~\ref{fig:binary_global_score}.

\subsection{Specialised model}
\label{sec:binary_specialised}

Three independent classifiers are trained, on $\mathcal{O}_{S,0}+\mathcal{O}_{S,1}$, $\mathcal{O}_{M,0}+\mathcal{O}_{M,1}$ and $\mathcal{O}_{T,0}+\mathcal{O}_{T,1}$ respectively. On the final configuration they reach AUCs of $0.994$, $0.992$ and $0.992$ when probed with the lowest-index operator of each family. The score distributions in Figure~\ref{fig:binary_spec_scores} share a common pattern: signal peaks sharply at $s\approx1$, background at $s\approx0$, with a clean bimodal separation that is qualitatively very similar across the three operator families despite their different polarisation structure. The corresponding ROC curves of Figure~\ref{fig:binary_spec_roc} sit close to the top-left corner in each case.

Tracing the iteration columns of Table~\ref{tab:binary_auc} shows two effects. The change of upstream tagger from version~2 to version~3 lifts the specialised AUC by 0.1\% to 0.3\% across the three families, modest but uniform. The equal signal cross-section reweighting moves the specialised AUC by less than $\pm 0.1\%$: the specialised training mixture only spans two operators in a single family, which already share a similar effective cross section, so the rebalancing has nothing to correct and therefore no headline AUC footprint.

These AUC values look near-perfect, but the AUC alone is misleading here. The signal and the QCD-dominated background are distinct physical processes and the discriminating information is concentrated at the extreme low and high ends of the score distribution where the network has no difficulty separating the two classes. Figure~\ref{fig:binary_spec_counts}, which repeats the specialised score distributions on an absolute event scale for the three families, makes the imbalance explicit: the QCD-dominated background produces $\mathcal{O}(10^5)$ weighted events at low score, whereas the per-family signal yields are many orders of magnitude smaller, so even in the highest-score bin the absolute background exceeds the signal by a factor of $\mathcal{O}(10^2)$ for the scalar and mixed probe operators and only approaches comparable yields for the high cross-section tensor probe operator. AUC, which weights all thresholds equally, therefore over-states what can be inferred about the analysis sensitivity. The genuine question, whether the cleaner v3 score shape is worth the doubled background yield it accepts, is decided by the Asimov significance in Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity_comparison}.

\subsection{Global model}
\label{sec:binary_global}

A single MLP is trained jointly on all available aQGC operators across the three families, $S(0\text{--}1) + M(0\text{--}5,\,7) + T(0\text{--}9)$. On the final configuration it reaches AUCs of $0.993$, $0.992$ and $0.991$ when probed with $S_0$, $M_0$ and $T_0$ respectively, so per family the global model loses at most $\approx 0.1\%$ AUC relative to its specialised counterpart. Carrying a single decision boundary across the entire operator space is therefore effectively free in AUC, while greatly reducing operational complexity.

Tracing the iteration columns of Table~\ref{tab:binary_auc} now shows a clear footprint for the equal signal cross-section reweighting. The global $S_0$ AUC rises by $+0.3\%$ at both upstream versions, from $0.986$ to $0.989$ at v2 and from $0.990$ to $0.993$ at v3, while the $M_0$ and $T_0$ AUCs are unchanged within rounding. The global binary classifier collapses every aQGC operator into a single signal category, so within that category the high cross-section tensor (and to a lesser extent mixed) operators dominate the optimisation and the scalar operators contribute relatively little. The equal signal cross-section reweighting restores their contribution at training time, recovering the scalar classification at no cost to the mixed or tensor families.

The score distribution for the final configuration, Figure~\ref{fig:binary_global_score}, makes the operator-inclusive behaviour explicit: $S_0$, $M_0$ and $T_0$ peak together near $s\approx1$ and lie close enough to one another that a single score generalises well across the three families. The absolute-scale counterpart, Figure~\ref{fig:binary_global_counts}, repeats the AUC caveat already encountered for the specialised models, the AUC is not a useful proxy for the expected sensitivity. As for the specialised models, the genuine performance comparison is deferred to Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity_comparison}.

\section{Multi-class classification}
\label{sec:multiclass_classification}

The binary classifiers of Section~\ref{sec:binary_classification} reduce every event to a single signal versus background score and discard any information about which operator family is responsible. The multi-class architecture introduced in Section~\ref{sec:mldiscriminant} keeps that information by replacing the single sigmoid output with a four-node softmax over $\{\,\mathrm{background},\,S,\,M,\,T\,\}$, trained on every available aQGC operator simultaneously. The behaviour of the four output nodes on the v3 + equal-$\sigma$ configuration is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:multiclass_scores}. The background node behaves as a clean signal versus background discriminant, mirroring the global binary model with $S_0$, $M_0$ and $T_0$ piling up at low $\hat{y}_{\mathrm{bkg}}$ and the composite background at high $\hat{y}_{\mathrm{bkg}}$. The scalar node separates the scalar family well: $S_0$ peaks at high $\hat{y}_S$, the $M_0$ and $T_0$ sit at intermediate values and the background decreases towards low $\hat{y}_S$. The mixed and tensor nodes, however, are essentially degenerate, $M_0$ and $T_0$ peak together at the same $\hat{y}_M$ and $\hat{y}_T$ values, with $S_0$ and background sitting below in both, so neither node can reliably tell the two operator families apart from the input features used here.

The equal signal cross-section reweighting is most consequential for this classifier, but for a different reason than for the global binary model of Section~\ref{sec:binary_global}. The four softmax categories $\{\,\mathrm{background},S,M,T\,\}$ are already class-balanced at training time, so the imbalance the reweighting addresses sits one level deeper, inside each of the three signal categories. The scalar category combines $S_0$ and $S_1$ at widely different cross sections, the mixed category covers $M(0$ to $5,\,7)$ over roughly two orders of magnitude in cross section and the tensor category covers $T(0$ to $9)$ over a similar dynamic range. Within every operator the three diboson decay channels ($WW$, $WZ$, $ZZ$) contribute with different cross sections as well. Without rebalancing, the network learns each category from its highest cross-section operators and channels and under-fits the rare ones. The improvement is uniform across the two upstream tagger versions, so the reweighting is best understood as an orthogonal training-time fix rather than a v3-specific correction. The physical cross sections are restored at evaluation.

Even with the $M/T$ degeneracy, this architecture is potentially powerful for the analysis. The four output nodes provide four complementary discriminating directions from a single trained model, so the multi-class classifier can supply a different best-separating discriminant for each probed operator without retraining: the scalar node strongly separates $S$, the mixed and tensor nodes both still separate their operators from background and the background node provides a generic signal versus background score. Whether this multiplicity of discriminants translates into a sensitivity gain over the global binary classifier is decided again by the Asimov significance reported in Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity_comparison}.

The four-way classification can also be summarised by the confusion matrix of Figure~\ref{fig:multiclass_cm}, obtained by assigning each event to the softmax node with the highest output and normalising by true class. The background is recovered cleanly ($94\%$) and the scalar family reasonably well ($78\%$), but the mixed and tensor classes are heavily cross-assigned: only $22\%$ of true mixed events are labelled mixed, with $32\%$ migrating to the scalar and $42\%$ to the tensor node and true tensor events are recovered with only $56\%$ efficiency. This is the confusion-matrix manifestation of the mixed/tensor degeneracy already seen in the output node distributions of Figure~\ref{fig:multiclass_scores}.

The confusion matrix is, however, not the best gauge of performance for this analysis, for the same reason discussed above for the output node distributions. It forces a hard four-way argmax assignment over $\{\,\mathrm{background},S,M,T\,\}$, whereas the multi-class discriminant is never used that way: the analysis does not employ multiple signal output nodes simultaneously, but probes one operator at a time, retaining only the output node matching the family of the probed operator and using it, together with the background, as a signal versus background discriminant.

\section{Feature ranking}
\label{sec:feature_ranking}

The MLP discriminants of the previous sections each take the twelve event-level input features defined in Section~\ref{sec:mldiscriminant}: the transverse momentum, pseudorapidity, azimuthal angle and invariant mass of each of the two leading large-$R$ signal jets, the VBF-RNN score and three multiplicity variables, the number of RNN tagging jets, the total number of small-$R$ jets and the number of large-$R$ jets. These inputs do not contribute equally to the learnt decision boundary. Quantifying their relative contribution serves two purposes: it provides a physical cross-check that the network relies on the kinematic handles expected to carry the aQGC information and it identifies redundant inputs that carry little or no weight and could in principle be dropped.

The relative contribution of each input is quantified with the permutation importance method introduced in Section~\ref{sec:permutation_importance}: each feature is rendered uninformative in turn by randomly permuting its values across the validation sample and the resulting drop in performance measures how strongly the trained network relies on it. The performance metric is the AUC defined in Section~\ref{sec:vbfrnn_eval} and the relative feature importance $I_j$ of Equation~\ref{eq:rel_importance} is the per-feature AUC drop normalised to unit sum. Where a ROC curve is shown, each ``without feature $X$'' curve is the ROC obtained on the corresponding permuted sample $\mathcal{D}^{\pi_j}$, its area being the degraded metric $A_j$. Because the network is not retrained, a feature whose information is also carried by a correlated partner can be permuted with little loss and is then assigned a low importance. This correlated-feature masking is examined further in the correlation studies of Section~\ref{sec:correlation_studies}. The full set of per-classifier importance bar charts and per-feature ROC curves, including the six one versus one tasks of the multi-class architecture, is reproduced in Appendix~\ref{app:featureranking}.

\subsection{Specialised models}
\label{sec:fr_specialised}

The permutation importance of the three specialised binary classifiers is shown in Table~\ref{tab:fr_rank_spec}. All three models behave the same way at the coarsest level: the large-$R$ signal-jet kinematics dominate, while the two azimuthal angles $\phi$ carry no importance whatsoever and the pseudorapidities and the small-$R$/large-$R$ jet multiplicities contribute at most a per cent each. The vanishing $\phi$ importance is expected from the azimuthal symmetry of the detector, which makes the $\phi$ distributions flat for both signal and background (Figure~\ref{fig:input_distributions}), these inputs could be dropped from the discriminant with no loss of performance.

The informative variable dependence lies in how the importance is distributed among the signal-jet kinematics and it tracks the polarisation structure of the operator families (Chapter~\ref{chap:sm}). For the scalar classifier the two signal-jet masses dominate, jointly carrying $54\%$ of the importance, with the leading-jet $p_T$ third at $18\%$. For the mixed and tensor classifiers the ordering is reversed: the leading signal-jet $p_T$ alone carries $45\%$ and $57\%$ respectively and the jet masses are demoted. The mixed and tensor operators enhance the high-energy tail of the diboson system strongly, so the leading-jet $p_T$ becomes the single most powerful handle, whereas the scalar operators produce a milder $p_T$ enhancement and the discrimination leans instead on the boson-jet mass, which peaks in the $W/Z$ mass window for signal. The VBF-RNN score is a modest contributor ($11\%$ for the scalar model, 2\% to 3\% for the transverse families): it is itself a strong tagger, but the training selection already requires a score above $0.3$, so the surviving events are RNN-enriched.

Table~\ref{tab:fr_specialised_auc} shows that the classifiers are robust to the loss of any single feature: the baseline AUC, between 0.991 and 0.994, falls by at most $\approx0.02$ for the most important input and is unchanged to three decimals when a $\phi$ variable is removed. The largest single drop is for the leading-jet $p_T$ in the tensor model ($0.991\to0.933$) and for the leading-jet mass in the scalar model ($0.994\to0.972$), consistent with the importance ordering.

\subsection{Global model}
\label{sec:fr_global}

The global binary classifier is trained jointly on all aQGC operators across the three families. Its permutation importance, Figure~\ref{fig:fr_rank_global}, is intermediate between the scalar and the transverse specialised models: the leading signal-jet $p_T$ is the single most important feature at $41\%$, but the two jet masses together still account for $39\%$. This reflects the composition of the joint training set, $S(0\text{--}1)+M(0\text{--}5,\,7)+T(0\text{--}9)$, which is numerically dominated by transverse operators, so the $p_T$-driven transverse behaviour prevails, while the equal signal cross-section reweighting restores the scalar contribution and keeps the jet masses relevant, in line with the discussion of Section~\ref{sec:binary_global}. The remaining inputs follow the specialised pattern: the number of RNN jets contributes $8\%$, the VBF-RNN score and the sub-leading $p_T$ about $4\%$ each and the $\eta$, $\phi$ and small-/large-$R$ multiplicity variables are negligible. As for the specialised models the classifier is robust to the loss of any single input: the baseline AUC of $0.991$ degrades by at most $0.036$, when the leading signal-jet $p_T$ is permuted and is unchanged when a $\phi$ variable is removed (Table~\ref{tab:fr_global_auc} and Figure~\ref{fig:fr_roc_global}).

\subsection{Multi-class model}
\label{sec:fr_multiclass}

For the multi-class classifier the permutation importance is evaluated separately for each pair of classes: the validation sample is restricted to the two classes in question and the metric $A_0$ in Equation~\ref{eq:rel_importance} is the corresponding one versus one AUC. The six pairs split naturally into the three signal versus background tasks and the three inter-signal tasks and the two groups behave very differently.

The signal versus background rankings, Table~\ref{tab:fr_rank_mc_bkg}, reproduce the specialised and global results almost exactly: the scalar versus background axis is driven by the jet masses, the mixed- and tensor versus background axes by the leading signal-jet $p_T$ and the one versus one AUCs ($0.994$, $0.991$, $0.991$) match those of the dedicated binary classifiers. This is a strong consistency check that the four-node softmax learns the same signal versus background physics as the binary models while additionally providing the operator-family information.

The inter-signal tasks, Table~\ref{tab:fr_rank_mc_sig}, are a different matter. Their baseline AUCs are much lower (Figure~\ref{fig:fr_roc_mc_sig}), $0.841$ for tensor versus scalar, $0.804$ for mixed versus scalar and only $0.602$ for tensor versus mixed, the last being the feature-ranking manifestation of the mixed/tensor degeneracy already seen in the output node distributions (Figure~\ref{fig:multiclass_scores}) and the confusion matrix (Figure~\ref{fig:multiclass_cm}). The variable dependence also changes. The jet masses, which dominate the signal versus background separation, lose almost all importance, because the two transverse families share essentially the same boson content and the mass therefore cannot distinguish them. Discrimination shifts onto the leading signal-jet $p_T$ together with the sub-leading jet $p_T$ and the number of large-$R$ jets. For the scalar versus transverse tasks a dominant handle still exists, the leading-jet $p_T$ carrying 40\% to 47\%. For tensor versus mixed the importance profile is essentially flat: the leading-jet $p_T$ falls to $29\%$, the jet multiplicities ($16\%$ and $14\%$ for the number of RNN and large-$R$ jets) and even the pseudorapidities ($\approx10\%$ each) become comparably important. A flat ranking with no dominant feature is exactly what is expected when two classes are nearly inseparable and Table~\ref{tab:fr_mc_auc} confirms it quantitatively: removing the leading-jet $p_T$ lowers the tensor versus mixed AUC only from $0.602$ to $0.570$, so the modest residual separation is spread thinly across many weak handles rather than concentrated in any one of them. As in every other model the $\phi$ variables remain uninformative throughout.

\section{Correlation studies}
\label{sec:correlation_studies}

The feature ranking of Section~\ref{sec:feature_ranking} quantifies how strongly the trained network relies on each input, but it does not show how the discriminant depends on the underlying event kinematics, nor whether the network has access to physics beyond the literal list of twelve inputs. This section addresses both questions by examining the two-dimensional distribution of the binary discriminant $\ln[p/(1-p)]$ against selected kinematic variables. The distributions are shown for the global binary classifier on the v3~+~equal-$\sigma$ configuration, separately for the composite background and for the three probe operators $S_0$, $M_0$ and $T_0$, each panel normalised independently so that the colour encodes the shape of the joint density rather than the absolute yield. The distributions against the remaining input variables, including the VBF-RNN score and the sub-leading signal-jet observables and the equivalent distributions for the multi-class scalar-node discriminant, are reproduced in Appendix~\ref{app:correlations}.

Figure~\ref{fig:corr_disc_pt} shows the discriminant against the leading signal-jet $p_T$. In every panel the two quantities are strongly and positively correlated: the populated region forms a diagonal band and the lower-right corner, a high $p_T$ paired with a low, background-like discriminant, is essentially empty. The correlation is present not only across the signal panels but within the background panel itself: a hard leading jet pulls a background event towards a higher, more signal-like score irrespective of its truth label. This is the physically desired behaviour. The aQGC operators enhance the high-energy tail of the central diboson system (Chapter~\ref{chap:sm}), so a large leading-jet $p_T$ is genuinely signal-like and a background event that happens to populate that tail is correctly assigned a signal-like score.

A more subtle question is whether the network is limited to the variables it is explicitly given. The twelve inputs of Section~\ref{sec:mldiscriminant} include the $p_T$, $\eta$, $\phi$ and mass of each of the two leading signal jets individually, but not the invariant mass $m_{JJ}$ of the two-jet system. Figure~\ref{fig:corr_disc_mjj} shows the discriminant against $m_{JJ}$ nonetheless and it displays the same pronounced diagonal correlation as the leading-jet $p_T$, in every panel including the background. This observation matters, because $m_{JJ}$ is the baseline discriminant against which the analysis sensitivity is normalised (Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity_comparison}), so the MLP captures the information of the baseline discriminant without ever being used as an input.

The leading signal-jet $\eta$ provides the counter-example. Figure~\ref{fig:corr_disc_eta} shows that the discriminant is essentially flat in $\eta$: at any fixed value of $\ln[p/(1-p)]$ the density is uniform across the full $|\eta|<2$ range and the background and signal panels differ only in the vertical, discriminant direction, not in their $\eta$ dependence. There is no diagonal structure of the kind seen for $p_T$ and $m_{JJ}$, because the leading-jet $\eta$ distribution of the signal barely differs from that of the background: the aQGC operators modify the energy and mass scale of the central diboson system, not its rapidity, so $\eta$ carries almost no class-separating information. This is the kinematic explanation of the $\approx1\%$ permutation importance assigned to the signal-jet pseudorapidities in Table~\ref{tab:fr_rank_spec} and Figure~\ref{fig:fr_rank_global}. Taken together, the three distributions confirm that the discriminant is built on the physically motivated handles, the $p_T$ and the implicitly reconstructed $m_{JJ}$ of the central system and is correctly insensitive to variables such as $\eta$ that do not separate the aQGC signal from the background.

\begin{figure}[htbp]

\section{Sensitivity comparison}
\label{sec:sensitivity_comparison}

The AUC values, ROC curves, output-node distributions and confusion matrices of the previous sections establish that the binary and multi-class discriminants all separate aQGC signal from QCD background cleanly, but they do not by themselves rank the model types as analysis discriminants. The metric that probes precisely that is the binned Asimov significance $Z_A$ defined in Section~\ref{sec:mldiscriminant}, computed with a per-bin Gaussian background uncertainty $\sigma_{b,i}=0.30\,b_i$ as introduced there. The following subsections evaluate $Z_A$ operator by operator, separately for the four iterations of the upstream configuration: VBF-RNN version~2 (4-jet input) and version~3 (6-jet input), each without and with the equal signal cross-section reweighting of Section~\ref{sec:mldiscriminant}. To enable a meaningful comparison between operators that differ in cross section by up to two orders of magnitude, every Asimov significance is reported both as an absolute number and normalised to the corresponding $m_{JJ}$-only baseline $Z_A^{m_{JJ}}$, computed on the same training selection and with the same binning prescription and background-uncertainty model.

Section~\ref{sec:sens_discriminants} first inspects the per-bin shape of the four candidate discriminants on the final v3~+~equal-$\sigma$ configuration. The three remaining subsections, organised by operator family, then present the per-operator significance heatmaps on that configuration together with tables that quantify how each model gains or loses absolute significance going from v2 to v3~+~equal-$\sigma$. Section~\ref{sec:sens_iteration} summarises the cross-family observations.

\subsection{Scalar-type operators}
\label{sec:sens_scalar}

The scalar family has the smallest signal cross section of the three: at the v3 training selection the inclusive cut and count significance for $S_0$ is consistent with zero and the $m_{JJ}$-only fit produces $Z_A^{m_{JJ}}=0.004$, both well below unity. Any meaningful sensitivity must therefore come from the multivariate discriminant, which makes this family the most informative test of the three model types.

The relative to specialised heatmap of Figure~\ref{fig:sens_hm_S}(c) shows that the global binary classifier loses $29\%$ to $32\%$ of the specialised significance on this family and the multi-class classifier $25\%$ to $27\%$. The global classifier is affected by the operator-mixture imbalance discussed in Section~\ref{sec:binary_global}: when the network is trained on every operator simultaneously, the high cross-section tensor and mixed operators dominate the gradient and the rare scalar operators receive too little training weight. The equal-$\sigma$ reweighting partially compensates for this, but the residual gap remains visible here. The multi-class classifier mitigates the problem because its softmax already balances the three signal categories at the top level, leaving the within-category imbalance to be addressed by the equal-$\sigma$ reweighting alone.

The reweighting is most clearly illustrated by the v2 to v3~+~equal-$\sigma$ comparison in Table~\ref{tab:sens_gain_S}. The specialised classifier gains $13\%$ to $14\%$ in absolute $Z_A$, while the global and multi-class classifiers gain $35\%$ to $44\%$. The specialised gain is essentially the upstream-tagger contribution alone. The much larger global and multi-class gains are dominated by the reweighting, because for those architectures the scalar family was being suppressed at training time.

The smaller ratio relative to the scalar and mixed families is a direct consequence of the larger absolute $m_{JJ}$ baseline on the tensor family: the tensor operators populate the high-mass tail more strongly than the scalar and mixed operators, so the $m_{JJ}$-only fit retains some discriminating information even under the $30\%$ background uncertainty. The most important observation from the relative to specialised panel is that on the v3~+~equal-$\sigma$ configuration the multi-class classifier tracks the binary models closely on the tensor family, between $0.79$ and $0.93$, after being severely degraded on every earlier iteration. The equal-$\sigma$ reweighting, addressing the cross-section spread within the tensor category at training time, is exactly the fix needed to allow the multi-class classifier to compete with the dedicated binary models.

Table~\ref{tab:sens_gain_T} quantifies the v2 to v3~+~equal-$\sigma$ change for the tensor family. Unlike the scalar and mixed families, the specialised classifier here loses a substantial fraction of absolute $Z_A$ on going from v2 to v3~+~equal-$\sigma$. The loss is largest ($-26\%$ to $-27\%$) for the rare $T_4$ and $T_7$ operators. The cause is the larger background admitted by the v3 selection, which the specialised classifier, trained on only two tensor operators and therefore not benefiting from the equal-$\sigma$ rebalancing, cannot fully recover from. The global binary classifier gains $4\%$ to $6\%$ on the same comparison and the multi-class classifier gains $18\%$ to $42\%$, again with the largest gains on the rarest operators. The three trends taken together identify the equal-$\sigma$ reweighting as the dominant change between v2 and v3~+~equal-$\sigma$ for any architecture that mixes operators at training time, while the upstream tagger choice itself penalises the specialised classifier.

\subsection{Comparison across upstream configurations}
\label{sec:sens_iteration}

The three per-family scans, taken together, allow several cross-family observations on the iteration v2 $\to$ v3~+~equal-$\sigma$.

The upstream tagger change, from VBF-RNN v2 (4-jet input) to v3 (6-jet input), penalises the specialised classifier on the tensor family by $14\%$ to $27\%$ in absolute $Z_A$, while gaining $13\%$ to $14\%$ on the scalar family and remaining flat on the mixed family. The pattern is consistent with the v3 selection admitting more inclusive background than v2, which (i) tightens the per-bin background statistics and helps the rare-signal scalar operators where the discriminant is information-limited, but (ii) degrades the high-$Z_A$ tensor operators because the absolute background growth at v3 outweighs any classifier gain. The same pattern is visible in the $m_{JJ}$-only baseline: $Z_A^{m_{JJ}}$ for $T_0$ falls from $12.6$ at v2 to $7.2$ at v3, while $Z_A^{m_{JJ}}$ on $S_0$ falls from $0.011$ to $0.004$. The large relative to $m_{JJ}$ gains seen for v3 in the normalised heatmaps are therefore partly a baseline effect: the suppressed $m_{JJ}$ significance inflates the ratio for any multivariate discriminant.

The equal-$\sigma$ reweighting is, by contrast, the dominant positive change for any architecture that mixes operators at training time. The global binary classifier gains $40\%$ to $44\%$ on the scalar family, $7\%$ to $11\%$ on the mixed family and $4\%$ to $6\%$ on the tensor family. The multi-class classifier gains $35\%$ to $41\%$, $32\%$ to $50\%$ and $18\%$ to $42\%$ on the same three families. In every case the gain is largest on the rarest operators, exactly as expected from the cross-section diagnostic of Section~\ref{sec:mldiscriminant}: when the loss is dominated by the high cross-section operators of each category, the rare operators are effectively absent from the optimisation and the trained network is sub-optimal on them. The equal-$\sigma$ reweighting redistributes the training weight uniformly within each category, restoring sensitivity to the rare operators without measurable harm to the abundant ones. The specialised classifier, trained on only two operators of a single family that already have similar effective cross sections, has nothing to rebalance and is correspondingly insensitive to the reweighting.

The three architectures finish the iteration in the following positions on v3~+~equal-$\sigma$. The specialised classifier is the strongest of the three on the scalar and mixed families, reaching $Z_A=1.22$ ($S_0$) and $11.0$ ($M_0$). On the tensor family the global binary classifier overtakes the specialised one, reaching $Z_A=94.2$ on $T_0$ versus $80.8$ for the specialised classifier. The global binary classifier is within $\pm 5\%$ of the specialised classifier on the mixed family and $29\%$ to $32\%$ below on the scalar family. The multi-class classifier sits at $0.73$ to $0.75$ of the specialised on the scalar family, $0.61$ to $0.69$ on the mixed family and $0.79$ to $0.93$ on the tensor family.

Which of the three is adopted as the final analysis discriminant is not decided in this thesis. The specialised classifier achieves the largest absolute $Z_A$ on the scalar and mixed families but requires three independently trained networks. The global binary classifier provides a single model covering all $19$ probed operators at the cost of $29\%$ to $32\%$ on the scalar family and is the best architecture on the tensor family. The multi-class classifier additionally exposes the operator-family identity in its output but pays a further $7\%$ to $21\%$ on the tensor family, $31\%$ to $39\%$ on the mixed family and $25\%$ to $27\%$ on the scalar family. The trade-off between absolute sensitivity, model coverage and operator-family identification must inform the final choice in the analysis proper.

What can be concluded from the present comparison is more specific. Firstly, every multivariate discriminant studied here outperforms the $m_{JJ}$-only baseline by factors between $11$ and $281$ in $Z_A$ depending on the operator and architecture, with the largest gains on the small cross-section scalar family and the smallest on the tensor family that already populates the high-$m_{JJ}$ tail strongly. Secondly, the equal-$\sigma$ reweighting is essential for any classifier that mixes operators at training time: it is the dominant positive change between the v2 and the final v3~+~equal-$\sigma$ configurations for the global and multi-class classifiers (Tables~\ref{tab:sens_gain_S}--\ref{tab:sens_gain_T}) and only with it does the multi-class classifier become competitive with the specialised model on the tensor family (Figure~\ref{fig:sens_hm_T}). Thirdly, the choice of upstream tagger between v2 and v3 is not neutral once the background uncertainty is included: the specialised classifier penalty on the tensor family disfavours v3 for the specialised architecture, while the equal-$\sigma$ reweighting more than compensates the loss for the global and multi-class architectures. The choice is therefore tied to the discriminant architecture and the orthogonal physics considerations of Sections~\ref{sec:vbfrnn_perf} and~\ref{sec:vbfrnn_aqgc}.
